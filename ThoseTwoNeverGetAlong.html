In a crowded office building, the day was ripe with merriment. Despite the workplace’s usual monotony, today was an exception—a burst of warmth and levity in an otherwise draining environment.

February fourteenth, twenty twenty five.

The scent of chocolate mixed with the sterile tang of printer ink. Roses sat on desks, nestled between stacks of reports, and colorful greeting cards peeked out from keyboards. Laughter echoed softly between cubicles, as coworkers exchanged small gifts and well-wishes. Even the harshest deadlines felt a little less suffocating.

But not everyone had been swept up in the cheerful camaraderie.

“You missed something,” Theo remarked, dropping a stack of papers onto a desk with a dull thud.

Lena glanced up from her monitor, eyes narrowing. “You could have just emailed me.”

“And you could have caught your mistake before I had to fix it,” Theo shot back, arms crossed. “But you didn’t. I guess you’re a magnet for mistakes.”

“One missing data entry is not the end of the world, asshole,” Lena snapped, grabbing the papers with an exaggerated roll of her eyes.

“That’s very unprofessional language,” Theo countered smoothly. “I could write you up for that.”

“You wouldn’t.”

“You sound awfully sure of that.”

“I am.”

Around them, coworkers exchanged knowing glances. These two—Theo and Lena—were infamous, their arguments a reliable fixture in the office landscape. Where there was one, the other was never far behind, their sharp words bouncing off each other relentlessly.

“Those two never get along,” someone muttered.

But that was not true at all. They got along better than anyone there.

At the end of the day, Theo and Lena left the office, still glaring at each other.

They entered the subway station, still glaring at each other.

They sat on the train, steadfastly ignoring each other.

A nearby workmate noticed them and chuckled. “They can’t even leave the office without hating each other. I wonder where they’re going.”

But that was not true at all. And they were going to the exact same place.

It was quiet and peaceful that night. But it was only 5:30. The night was still young.

Lena walked into a modern-looking condo, setting her bag down before heading straight for the kitchen. She opened the fridge and retrieved a small, neatly wrapped box of chocolates.

Seconds later, Theo walked into a modern-looking condo, setting down his bag and turning to the kitchen to see the fridge open. Lena was standing there with a smile on her face. He sauntered over, his tired expression turning into a smirk.

“You were particularly ruthless today,” he said, chuckling.

“I may have gotten a little carried away…” Lena replied sheepishly.

“A little?” Theo hugged her from behind. “You called me an asshole.”

Lena shrunk into his arms shyly. “I didn’t mean it.”

“Really?”

“Really,” Lena nodded numbly. “I’m sorry.”

“Dummy.” He ruffled her hair with affection.

Lena pouted before smiling. “Happy Valentine’s,” she said, turning in his arms and holding up the box. “I made these for you.”

Theo accepted the gift, opening it with genuine delight. He popped a chocolate into his mouth, savoring it. “I did the same,” he admitted, reaching into the fridge to retrieve an identical box.

Lena grinned. “Great minds think alike.” She took a piece and bit into it. Immediately, her face lit up. “Oh! It’s dark chocolate.”

“Just the way you like it.”

Her smile softened, and she leaned into him. “Did you prepare the wine?”

“It’s in the cooler outside.”

Lena sighed, stretching. “Man, I’m so tired. I just want to go out and relax.”

Theo chuckled. “Shall we, then?”

She smirked. “Of course.”

This was how they truly were. But no one at the workplace would know that.

The motor of a balcony hot tub rumbled, as Lena and Theo sat inside. They smiled at each other, clinked their glasses together, took a sip of wine each, and then kissed under the moonlight.

“Has your dad responded yet?” she asked, her voice soft but probing.

Theo’s smirk faded slightly. “He keeps dodging the subject every time I bring up marriage.”

Lena sighed. “I thought we had gotten through to him.”

“You know how particular he is,” Theo muttered, rubbing his temple. “Remember how long it took just for him to accept us living together?”

“Well, I don’t really mind it,” Lena said with a smile, kissing him again, before leaning against him. “I can wait for as long as it takes.”

Theo smiled. “I love you.”

Lena smiled back. “That’s obvious.”

“Aw, I was hoping you would say it back,” Theo said playfully.

“Do I have to?” Lena asked teasingly.

“It would be appreciated.”

Lena laughed. “Dummy, you already know how much I love you,” she said, leaning in to kiss him once more.

They kissed for a while, the cool nighttime air washing over them as their Valentine’s Day rendezvous continued…

They got along very well. Only they knew it, but it was fine that way.
