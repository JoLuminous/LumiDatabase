Play Pretend

The afternoon sun peeked through the blinds of Kikuri Hiroi’s messy apartment, casting slivers of light that rudely disrupted her from her sleep.

Groaning, Kikuri slowly opened her eyes, feeling the familiar pounding headache of another day spent drowning her miserableness in alcohol.

“…Fuck.”

She rubbed her head and tried to sit up, but the room started to spin violently, forcing her to fall back down.

“Ugh…not again,” she muttered, the taste of sake still strong in her mouth.

She needed to get up, staying in bed all day wasn’t healthy, especially for someone like her, but she couldn’t move. Her legs wouldn’t budge.

Her head felt like it was going to split open, as if someone was banging a gong continuously in her mind.

Why was life so hard? Why did she have to suffer this fate day after day in order to survive?

Eventually, after what felt like an eternity, she swung her legs over the side of the bed and forced herself to stand.

Stumbling a little, Kikuri attempted to make her way to the bathroom. All around her, empty sake boxes and old pieces of trash were scattered about, as if someone had thrown them carelessly.

“I’ll…clean this eventually…” she muttered, reaching the bathroom sink. She splashed cold water on herself in an attempt to wash away her drowsiness.

Kikuri stopped and looked in the mirror. There were deep bags under her eyes and her hair was a mess.

To be fair, her entire being was messy.

Kikuri chuckled sourly. “What a terrible face…”

Her phone buzzed on the sink counter, the screen lighting up with a message from one of her Sick Hack band members, Shima Iwashita.

Sighing, she picked it up and read the terse text: “Kikuri, you missed practice yesterday. Get your act together!”

“Yeah, yeah…whatever you say,” Kikuri mumbled, though no one was there to hear her.

She tied up her hair and threw on a jacket, making herself look somewhat normal. Then she forced a smile, though in reality she wanted to frown.

Shima was right, of course. She was a hot mess and needed to get things in order, but how could she?

It wasn’t easy for Kikuri to reach this point, where people had some sense of interest in her.

If she did improve herself, well…

Kikuri shook her head, walking along the bustling roads. It wasn’t worth thinking about such topics, not when the outcome was already obvious.

She stopped into a convenience store and purchased a small box of sake, taking a sip with an expression that was both despondent and contained a fake smile.

Why was she pretending to smile? Even Kikuri didn’t have a concrete answer, but perhaps it was the only way she could justify alcohol to herself.

She was smiling, so didn’t that mean it was fine? 

Not really, but she still needed the flawed justification anyway. She just had to pretend it was perfectly flawless, and it was.

For her, at least.

The familiar neon sign of Starry greeted Kikuri as she descended the club steps. As she pushed the door open, the sound of instruments reached her ears, drifting out in a cacophony of melodies and song.

Kikuri felt a pang of longing and sadness. She missed the days when music was just a fun pastime, an activity that brought her joy despite her loneliness.

These days, music seemed to be another factor that added to her anxiety and stress. She still loved it, but something had changed…or maybe she was the one who changed.

“You’re here again?” Seika’s voice cut through her thoughts, sharp as ever. The older Ijichi sister sat at the bar, arms crossed in disapproval.

Kikuri frowned. Seika was one of her longtime friends, and she was always strict, always critical of her reckless actions to the highest degree. At the same time, she was also one of the few people who cared about the bassist, even if she had trouble showing it.

“Where else would I be?” Kikuri muttered.

Seika noticed there was something off about her and stared intently. “What’s with that tone?”

“It’s nothing,” Kikuri said nervously with a fake smile. “I had just had a rough night.”

Seika rolled her eyes. “Every night is a rough night for you,” she retorted. “You seriously need to get your act together, Kikuri. You’re a hot mess.”

Kikuri forced a laugh, the sound hollow even to her. “Yeah, yeah, I know. I’m working on it!”

Seika sighed, shaking her head with a hint of worry. “Whatever. Just…don’t cause any trouble, alright?”

Kikuri nodded, making her way to the bar. She found a seat at the corner to sit in, watching as Seika’s younger sister, Nijika, led her band, Zip Tie, through a routine practice session. Nijika was passionate about the success of her band, leading them with apt confidence that made Kikuri feel inadequate.

It was true that she was practically the poster girl for Sick Hack, but she didn’t really lead them all that well, did she? They just sort of attracted success because of her drunken antics.

Kikuri sighed and found herself looking towards Hitori Gotoh, Zip Tie’s second guitarist. The shy, introverted girl had come quite a long way since joining the band, but traces of her insecurity still remained, in ways that mirrored the bassist’s own.

Hitori looked up to her, Kikuri knew as much, and though they rarely spoke, there was a silent sense of camaraderie between them.

It was a miracle, really, that anyone looked up to her, of all people. What made her even worth looking up to?

She was a miserable, lonely, introverted, insecure, mess of a person. She made music, but only to mask her many faults and anxieties.

But then again, so was Hitori Gotoh.

During a break in practice, Hitori hesitated for a moment before approaching Kikuri. “Um, Hiroi-san…are you okay?” she asked nervously.

Kikuri forced another smile, though it felt even more fragile than before. “I’m fine, Bocchi-chan. Just a little tired, that’s all.”

Hitori’s brow furrowed slightly, but she decided not to press the issue any further. “Um…if you ever need to talk…I’m here…”

“Thanks,” Kikuri replied, touched by her concern. “But I’m okay. I really am.”

Hitori didn’t seem to believe her words, but she walked away, not wanting to upset her.

Kikuri frowned. Did Hitori see something?

She shook her head.

No, that wasn’t possible. Hitori was just concerned, that’s all. What was there to worry about?

Kikuri took another sip of her sake box, finding it to be empty. When did she drink the rest?

Was it unconsciously? Had she grown so accustomed to the feeling that she didn’t even notice? Kikuri laid her head down on the bar, groaning.

This was awful.

She knew it was awful.

She knew she was a disgusting person for allowing this to happen to her.

But it was now a part of her. Something she couldn’t get rid of, even if she tried.

And she certainly tried.

Kikuri held a hand over her right wrist, wincing.

That was a time she would keep to the back of her mind for as long as she lived. No one needed to know. No one did know.

She had to keep it that way.

Kikuri’s grip on her wrist tightened.

Did she have to keep it that way?

What if she was wrong? What if there was a way of getting past all this?

No, no, she couldn’t do that. Not when the resulting outcome would be so dreadful and annoying.

The pitiful stares, the constant worries and concerns, she didn’t want any of that.

It was better to be judged as a drunk, than be pitied as a depressed loser. It was, right?

Or maybe it wasn’t. Maybe she was an idiot, and just wanted to keep her alcohol. Maybe she just didn’t want to lose the tool that made her feel some small semblance of worth to people, even if it was hurting her in the process.

Kikuri chuckled coldly. What normal person would try to justify alcohol to themselves? No, she was far beyond being a ‘normal’ person.

She was an idiot, through and through. But not without reason. Not without consequence.

“...what an idiot.” she muttered to herself.

Seika overheard her and nodded. “Yes, you are.”

Kikuri looked up from her depressed stupor and pretended to be embarrassed. “Have you been watching me this whole time?”

“Of course. Who knows what you’ll do if I don’t.” Seika snapped, though there was a hint of care in her words.

“No one trusts me! Except for Bocchi-chan.” Kikuri mumbled with a sigh.

Seika rolled her eyes. “Probably because she’s too nervous to say otherwise.”

Kikuri pouted. “That’s mean! Now I’m upset. Buy me a drink, senpai!”

“Not happening.”

“Aww! Please?” Kikuri wined, her actions a mix of her true feelings and the act she was putting on.

“Nope.”

Kikuri frowned and laid her head back down on the bar.

Nijika, who had been watching their conversation, eyed Kikuri with mild disdain.

“Why do you always come here drunk?” she asked, frustration clear in her voice. “Don’t you have your own band to worry about?”

Kikuri shrugged, trying to repel their concerns with humor. “What can I say? Starry has the best company.”

Seika shook her head. “You can laugh everything off as a joke now, but when something serious happens, it’ll be too late. You need to take better care of yourself.”

Kikuri brushed it off, as she always did, but the comments stung and bit at her more than she would like to admit.

Please don’t worry about me.

She was irked by the concerned glances, the words of worry and advice.

But also, don’t see this as all I am.

She wished people would see past her faults, the drunken antics, everything. That was why Sick Hack was a respite from all of this. She could do whatever she wanted there without anyone judging her for it.

But the more she stayed there and basked in the freedom, the worse effect it had on her.

She became more reckless, more rock and roll, more…dangerous.

And the pain didn’t just go away. It multiplied further and further, until…

Kikuri clenched her wrist.

She didn’t want to think about it. Not now.

After practice ended, Kikuri lingered for a while longer, watching Zip Tie pack up their equipment. She could feel someone’s eyes on her and looked up, seeing Hitori staring intently.

The young guitarist took a deep breath and approached her nervously. “Hiroi-san…there’s something I wanted to ask you…”

Kikuri smiled, genuinely this time, and gestured to the empty seat next to her. “Sure, Bocchi-chan. What’s up?”

Hitori fidgeted with her hands, debating whether she should speak. “I…I just wanted to know…are you sure you’re okay?”

Kikuri’s face went blank and she turned away from her with a frown. “I’m fine, Bocchi-chan. Just dealing with…some stuff.” she said, her voice quiet and emotionless.

Hitori hesitated, then leaned in slightly, whispering so no one could hear: “I noticed…the scars on your arm. You really tried to…hurt yourself…?”

Kikuri’s breath caught in her throat, her heart pounding. She had been so careful, so particular with her movements in order to hide those scars. She never wanted anyone to see them, anyone to know what was behind them, anyone to judge her for creating them.

Yet Hitori noticed, and that left her feeling vulnerable, weak, exposed, like all the walls she built for herself had been demolished.

“You saw…that?” Kikuri whispered, her voice shaking.

“I…I’m not going to tell anyone, I just…I wanted you to know that someone is here for you. I know I’m not the best at talking, but…I care about you, Hiroi-san.”

The sincerity in Hitori’s words hit Kikuri hard like a tidal wave. Tears welled up in her eyes, but she wiped them away so no one would see. Her body was shaking violently, and everything she kept within herself was just begging to break free. But she couldn’t let it. Not now.

At the same time…someone like Hitori…would it hurt to rely on her? To trust her? To confide in her the deep secrets held inside her heart?

“Bocchi-chan, thank you,” Kikuri started, turning around with a sad smile. “But…sorry!”

Kikuri took a swig of alcohol and ran out of Club Starry in a rush, not stopping despite the words of those around her.

Seika watched the whole ordeal, unsure of what to make of it. What had happened to cause her to run like that? Was it something Hitori had said?

She looks over at Hitori, who had an unusual sad frown on her face. The girl was known for always being gloomy, but this expression was sadder than any Seika had ever seen.

What did they talk about?

Kikuri ran into her apartment and closed the door tightly. She surveyed the mess around her and sighed.

The empty sake boxes, the discarded food containers, and in a corner she tried to forget, a sharp tool she had once used to hurt herself. The memory made her stomach churn, her body shake, and she grabbed her wrist, feeling pain, even though the wound was long healed.

Lying down on her bed, Kikuri stared up at the ceiling, forcing herself to smile. “I’m fine,” she whispered. “There’s nothing wrong with me.”

But deep down she knew it was a lie. She was just deluding herself. Of course Seika and Nijika were right to be concerned about her. Of course Hitori was right about her.

Why was she so afraid to admit that?

Why couldn’t she stop pretending? She wasn’t fine. She wasn’t okay.

She was destroying herself. And she was scared of herself.

Playing pretend, phoning it in as a ‘skilled drunken bassist’ was fun for a while, but it couldn’t go on.

She didn’t want to be a drunken bassist.

She just wanted to be a bassist.

Kikuri reached for her phone and took a deep breath. She dialed a number and waited for an answer. Eventually, she heard the voice she wanted to hear. “...Hello? Hiroi-san?”

Kikuri smiled sadly and shakily held the phone up to her ear. Her smile was dropping, but that didn’t matter. This was a step she needed to take.

“Hello, Bocchi-chan. Let’s have a talk.”

Play Pretend 2

“So that’s my story,” Kikuri Hiroi said with a resigned smile. “Depressing, isn’t it?”

Hitori shook her head. “I understand,” she muttered. “Insecurity is…painful.”

Kikuri chuckled sadly. “See, this is why I feel so comfortable talking the truth with you, Bocchi-chan. You get me.”

Hitori sat quietly, her eyes full of empathy and understanding.

The morning sky was gray and the streets around them were eerily quiet. They sat on a pair of secluded street steps, and an overwhelming sense of heaviness lingered in the air, furthered by the unspoken emotions that bubbled beneath the bassist’s surface.

This was a side of her that Hitori never expected to see.

Kikuri frowned, her hands trembling as she took a sip from a water bottle.

“You know, it’s funny,” she said, her voice almost a whisper. “I always thought I would get better as I grew older, but as the years have gone by, I’ve found myself becoming worse. I still feel insecure and conscious of how others feel about me. No matter how much I try to hide it, I can’t help the fear that envelops me.”

Hitori nodded slowly, finding similarities in her words. “I…I always get scared too,” she admitted, her voice wavering. “Talking to people scares me…I don’t want to be judged…or hated.”

Kikuri stared at the ground, a distant look in her eyes. “You want to escape it all,” she whispered. “But it always haunts you. Alcohol can make things seem better, it can make you feel invincible from those truths, but in the end, it’s all a lie. A dangerous, destructive lie.”

The air fell silent again, the weight of Kikuri’s truth hanging in the air like an inescapable shadow.

Hitori could see the pain in her eyes, and it made her feel so sympathetic. She certainly wasn’t one to dole out overwhelming concern, but something about Kikuri just made her…sad.

“There was a time,” Kikuri started, her voice barely audible, “when I tried to get away from all this. I thought I could do it. I thought I could just…toss the awful habits and thoughts aside. I didn’t want to rely on the bitter drink, the false side of myself, the…unrealistic expectations my drunken side had set. But the anxiety, the fear…it was all too much. I felt like I was drowning under my own failures, insecurities, everything. And in that moment of darkness, when my hope was at its lowest, I…” She clutched her wrist, her fingers shakily tracing the faint scars that told Hitori everything she needed to know.

Hitori frowned, her heart aching. “...so…those scars…”

Kikuri nodded, her entire form shaking. “Yes. I was in a bad place. And I hate myself for letting things get that far. For allowing my fears to control me. I feel like an awful, terrible person.”

Hitori reached out, testing her own limits. She wasn’t used to doing these kinds of things at all, but it just…felt right, in the moment.

“Everyone feels weak, Kikuri-san,” she muttered. “And sometimes, we…do things we aren’t proud of…but that doesn’t make you a bad person…so, don’t feel sad. What matters, is you’re here…or something like that. I guess I’m not good at being encouraging…”

Tears welled up in Kikuri’s eyes, but she chuckled through them. “Haha. Thank you, Bocchi-chan,” she laughed, her voice soft. “You don’t have to worry. Your words mean more to me than you’ll ever know…they are what has led me to reveal all these things about myself…I’m even reconsidering my decision to drink.”

Hitori gave a small smile. “You’re a good musician, Kikuri-san…I think…you deserve to be happy.”  

Kikuri wiped her eyes and took Hitori in a big hug, surprising her. “You’re right, Bocchi-chan. And so is Senpai. Despite her toughness, she’s been right about so many things…I want to try to face my fears, without the alcohol,” she murmured. “It won’t be easy, but I have to believe that I can do it. I know I can…”

Hitori nodded. “...you can. I’ve been doing it,” she encouraged. “But I also…have a long way to go.”

Kikuri smiled. “You’re stronger than you give yourself credit for, Bocchi-chan. In the many years I’ve been struggling with my problems, you’re the only one who directly approached me with concern. Broaching a subject like this takes serious guts, so…” She placed a hand on Hitori’s head and rubbed her hair fondly. “Thanks for being brave, for my sake. I really appreciate it.”

Hitori blushed and giggled shamelessly. “It’s…it’s nothing, Kikuri-san…hehe.”

For a moment, they sat there in silence, the bond between them stronger as a result of their shared feelings. It was a moment of vulnerability, honesty, and of hope for a future in which they could face things head on without the insecurity and anxiety that plagued them.

A new chapter was beginning for both of them, and as long as they kept working towards the hopeful future they wanted so badly, they would one day succeed, together.

As the hour wore on, they talked more about their fears, their emotions, and their dreams. Kikuri told stories of her past, of the moments when she felt most alone. She shared times when everyone ignored her, when her efforts to find music oriented friends ended in solidarity, when the weight of the world felt at its highest.

“The first time I drank alcohol, I was actually underage,” she explained. “There was a party, and I wanted to fit in with everyone else.”

“Manager-san didn’t…try to stop you?” Hitori asked curiously.

“No, she encouraged me,” Kikuri said with a chuckle. “She told me that it would make me feel better. Those days, I was always depressed and lonely, even with Senpai at my side. Like always, she was just worried about me, and thought drinking would give me a boost. And well, it did succeed at that, I suppose…”

Hitori stared at Kikuri thoughtfully. “Are you and Manager-san on good terms?”

Kikuri nodded with a smile. “Of course we are,” she exclaimed. “I know she can be quite…rough, at times, and she has a harsh way of speaking, but she’s actually pretty sentimental. A lot of things she says are because she’s worried about people. She only yells at me because of my own faults. And she would never admit it, but she’s tough on you guys because she really cares about your band. She doesn’t want you to fail. But the problem is that she tries too hard to seem indifferent, so…she’s often misunderstood.”

Hitori frowned. “Well, I…I think she should try more…to help you…instead of yelling.”

“I agree,” Kikuri chuckled. “But try saying that to her face. It probably won’t go over well. You see, she’s sentimental, but that doesn’t mean she can’t get pissy.”

“Pfft–” Hitori covered her mouth to hide a giggle.

They both shared in each other’s feelings. Kikuri’s laughter, though still tinged with a bit of sadness, became more frequent as time went on.

Hitori shared her own stories of her struggles, the crippling anxiety that often left her falling apart, and the small moments of joy with her friends that kept her going and made her feel more confident.

She found solace in Kikuri’s presence, and Kikuri, in turn, found solace in hers. There was something about the fact that they shared the pain, the feelings of ineptitude and overwhelming fear.

Kikuri threw an arm around Hitori and smiled. “So, Kouhai, I see you brought your guitar like I asked.”

“Yes, I did,” Hitori nodded, glancing at the guitar case that lay next to her. “Why…?”

Kikuri picked up her bass and tossed it on her shoulder. “Well, what better way to deal with these heavy feelings than playing a bit of music, am I right?”

Hitori unzipped her case and pulled out her guitar. “What are we doing?” she asked curiously.

“How about some street performing? Like last time?” Kikuri suggested with a grin.

Hitori frowned. “I…I don’t know…I wasn’t prepared for this…”

Kikuri tapped her on the shoulder. “Come now, Kouhai. You’ve done plenty since then. I believe in you.”  

“A-alright…”

They walked together to a quiet spot on a nearby curb. It was a pretty active area, but not in the midst of the bustle of the city.

“When we did this before, you told me it helped a great deal. Maybe it can help me now,” Kikuri said, staring down at her bass.

They set up their instruments and began to play, their music filling the air. At first, only a few passersby stopped to listen, but over time a small crowd began to gather. Kikuri felt the familiar thrill of performing wash over her, yet the lack of alcohol and the presence of Hitori led to new feelings that she hadn’t experienced in what felt like years.

She wasn’t drunk, so why did this feel so exciting? So exhilarating?

Kikuri had forgotten this feeling, and now that she was experiencing it again, she felt emotional. How could she have lost sight of what made music so great? She didn’t need alcohol to feel this way, but she had become reliant on it. She had been ensnared, but now…the trap had finally been cut free.

Of course, it would take more than just a street performance and a few conversations to lead her to a proper recovery, but this was a good start.

Kikuri strummed her bass, the chords mingling with Hitori’s own to create a melody of emotion, soul, and, naturally, rock and roll.

They honestly felt like staying in that spot all day, taking in the gratifying feelings that surrounded them for a little while longer, but the end had to arrive eventually.  

As the crowd finally dispersed, Kikuri turned to Hitori, her eyes sparkling with genuine happiness and thankfulness.

“Thank you, Bocchi-chan. It’s been a while since I’ve felt happy doing this. I haven’t felt this good in a long time.”

Hitori shook her head profusely. “N-no…it wasn’t just me…this wouldn’t have happened if…you didn’t call me first.”

Kikuri chuckled. Impulsively, she leaned in and kissed Hitori on the cheek. “Thank you,” she whispered. “I’m going to work on myself. For your sake, Bocchi-chan.”

Hitori took her hand shyly, a flushed expression on her face. “I’ll help you…”

Kikuri smiled. “I know you will,” she said, hugging Hitori softly. “I know.”

“I hope she likes this,” Hitori Gotoh muttered as she walked down the dimly lit streets. Ahead, the bright neon lights of a nightclub were in view.

Her thoughts were on a notebook that lay within her bag, nestled amongst other belongings. This one object, simple as it was, had been plaguing her for the entire day. She had spent the previous few nights depriving herself of sleep in order to write two songs on its pages.

One was for Zip Tie, part of her usual work routine these days as she had chosen not to immediately attend college. The other song, however, was different–more personal, more heartfelt.

She wrote it for a single individual, someone she had come to care about as she grew older. Hitori would have never predicted she would feel this way about anyone in her life, but then again…was life ever predictable?

Her friends didn’t know about this, of course. They were friends, but she didn’t tell them everything about herself, even after they had been together for this long.

Naturally, she had no problem giving them the Zip Tie song, as it was simply created out of obligation and dedication to her band. But the other? She had written it in a subconscious moment of boldness, and now that it lay in her hands, she was tempted to throw it far away.

At the same time, she hesitated…

Kikuri would like it, wouldn’t she?

“Shit. Why is this so tough?” Hitori thought, grimacing hard.

She had become more confident in these two years, but why didn’t that also apply to her own courage? She could talk to others with more effort, but the thought of sharing embarrassing facets…

I really ought to tell Kikuri-san to reconsider…

But no, Hitori couldn’t do that.

She had come this far and invested so much time into preparing herself. Turning back was not an option anymore.

As Hitori approached the nightclub, she let out a tired sigh.

Ever since graduating high school, her days had become increasingly busy. Sure, she wasn’t suffering as much as the others, who had college to deal with, but writing music late into the night and working various day jobs was no easy task.

It was the support of her friends that kept her going these days. More specifically, it was the support 0f a certain person…

When Sick Hack’s set came to an end, the crowd erupted into applause and cheers. Kikuri entertained the audience with various rock moves, her eyes searching the room until they landed on Hitori. With a sly grin, she made her way off the stage and over to where Hitori was standing.

“Hey, you,” Kikuri greeted, pulling Hitori into a tight hug. “You never miss any of my performances, do you?”

Hitori turned away, embarrassed by the very public display of affection, but Kikuri wasn’t easily relenting. She pulled Hitori close with a teasing glint in her eye, whispering in her ear, “You know, if you keep making such cute faces, it’s going to be really hard for me to hold back.”

Hitori’s face flushed a deep shade of red, and she mumbled something incoherent, poorly attempting to hide her embarrassment. Kikuri laughed, planting a soft kiss on her cheek before letting her go.

“It’s so funny,” she whispered. “I’m not drinking, but I feel so drunk and giddy because of you.”

“S-stop it, Kikuri-san…” Hitori murmured. “I’m still not used to this.”

Kikuri pouted playfully. “Aw, but you were the one who made me like this, you know?”

From a distance, Seika watched the exchange with an expression that was both thoughtful and apprehensive. On one hand, she was glad to see Kikuri so genuinely happy after she had been acting for so long. At the same time, she was slightly irked by the age gap between them.

But despite all of that, she couldn’t deny the positive impact that they clearly had on each other.

Nijika, Kita, and Ryo had been watching too, and they were all blushing, their faces flushed with a mix of surprise and amusement. They had their suspicions, but this was the first time they had concrete confirmation of a relationship between the two…

“Did you see that?” Kita whispered excitedly. “They kissed! They’re totally in love!”

Ryo nodded, her usual stoic expression softened by their earnest love. “It’s quite unexpected, though.”

Nijika grinned, nudging Hitori with a playful look as she returned. “You two looked pretty cozy up there. Got something to share?”

Hitori stammered, her face still very bright red. “I-I don’t know what you’re talking about.”

Seika rolled her eyes with a smile. “Honestly, Bocchi-chan, it’s about time you two made things official.”

They all gathered around a small table near the bar, chatting about the performance and this surprising new relationship. Kikuri walked over to join them, her hand brushing against Hitori’s, a silent confirmation of their closeness.

“So, what next for Zip Tie, Nijika?” Seika asked, looking around at the group with genuine interest.

Nijika leaned back in her chair, a conflicted expression on her face. “It’s hard to say,” she said with a sigh. “We’re working on new material, but things have honestly been difficult, especially when balancing everything with college.”

Kita nodded in agreement. “It’s hard to find time to practice.”

“Yeah. I’ve been writing new songs for us,” Hitori added. “But I often get swamped with work as well.”

Seika took a sip of her drink. “There’s nothing wrong with taking a break once in a while,” she suggested. “Take care of your work first and foremost. Then you’ll have more time for Zip Tie.”

“Senpai’s right,” Kikuri said with a serious expression. “Music isn’t fun if you’re tired all the time. Trying to do both things at the same time is not a good idea.”

Seika eyed her in surprise and shook her head. “I’m not used to this responsible side of you, Kikuri. It’s very jarring.”

Kikuri chuckled. “Well, you know who to thank for that, right?”

Seika smiled, glancing at Hitori, who blushed. “Yes, I suppose I do.”

Kita looked at Kikuri and thought for a moment. “Say…Kikuri-san…if you were normal all this time, why did you hide it behind all the drunkenness?”

Kikuri and Hitori exchanged resigned smiles.

As the night drew to a close, Kikuri and Hiroi found themselves lingering outside the club. The others had already gone home, leaving the two musicians behind in their own company.

Kikuri wrapped an arm around Hitori’s shoulders, pulling her close. “You know,” she said softly, “I never thought I’d be this happy without alcohol. You’ve changed my life, Bocchi-chan.”

Hitori looked up at Kikuri, her eyes shining with many emotions. Love, appreciation, pride, happiness, all of those and much more. “You’ve changed mine too…”

Kikuri smiled and took her by the hand. “So, are you feeling bold tonight?”

“B-bold?” Hitori stuttered in shock.

“Well, I suppose I should treat you? For various different reasons.” Kikuri whispered with a sly grin.

Hitori blushed and looked to the ground. She muttered something Kikuri couldn’t quite hear.

“What did you say, Bocchi-chan? I couldn’t hear you.”

“I…I said…how far is your apartment from here?” Hitori mumbled.

Kikuri’s grin grew wider and she pulled the shy girl along. “Close enough,” she whispered.

Hitori leaned into Kikuri, feeling a warmth that went beyond the physical touch. Kikuri had revealed a new side of herself that was both surprising and liberating.

And at the same time, Hitori had also saved the bassist from her own demons, giving her a sense of pride in her actions that made her happy.

Tonight, she would show her heartfelt lyrics to Kikuri and see what she thought of them…

Hitori just hoped she would have the courage.

As they walked, the city and its sounds seemed to fade away, leaving just the two of them in their own world of love and camaraderie.

Kikuri eyed Hitori with a look of gratitude, feeling thankful for her breaking the chains that had been holding her down for so many years. Of course, the endless path of therapy sessions and rehab appointments were grueling and painful, but Hitori’s encouragement kept her going through all of it.

She wouldn’t stop trying. If not for herself, for Hitori Gotoh, who tried so hard for her sake.

Kikuri chuckled, thinking about something she hadn’t thought of in a long time.

When was the last time she put on a play?
