Second Chance Villainess

A story written by MirroredLuminescence.

Premise:

The villainess Rosalind drove her family to ruin as a result of her evil actions. Never once did she allow herself to feel a shred of guilt, nor did she think her poor choices were unwarranted. But the moment she sees her parents and siblings punished upon the blades of death, she feels a wave of anguish. Memories of the past appeared before her, reminding her of the people she used, the terrible schemes she hatched, and those she hurt. The pain of it all setting in, Rosalind wonders why she took this path. Why did she destroy herself and those she loved? As Rosalind faces her death in guilt-filled tears, she wishes for a second chance, for the opportunity to correct the terrible choices she’s made. The blade falls… but then she wakes up, her surroundings different, her age younger. Finding herself three years in the past, Rosalind decides to make a change. However, it won’t be easy for a notorious villainess to become someone else. Can Rosalind truly correct her mistakes of the past and save those she loves?

Drama, Regression, Time Travel, Romance, Hurt/Comfort, Self-Induced Trauma, Guilt, Friendship

Chapter 1 - The Curtain Falls

In all great love stories, there’s always a heroine and a villainess. The heroine, with her pure heart, rises above trials and hardships. The villainess, driven purely by jealousy, seeks to tear her down. In the end, the heroine prevails, and the villainess faces her inevitable demise, punished for the havoc she has wrought.

Rosalind Harcourt was one such villainess.

She thought herself invincible from all consequences, acting for what she perceived as her given right.

As the favored daughter of a powerful noble family and the consort of the crown prince, she believed it was perfectly fair that hindrances were dealt with. All the power in the world was at her command, of course she thought she could have everything she wanted. And when jealousy crept into her heart, it twisted her until all reasonable thought faded from her mind.

It was only natural, Rosalind thought. She had simply acted in the name of love.

But now, as she sat in a cold, damp prison cell, she wondered if she had ever truly known what love was. What good had it done her? What had her love for Prince Vicente brought her, aside from this never ending misery?

Rosalind stared dead-eyed through the iron bars of her prison. The walls around her were old and aging, most likely filled with dirt and grime from years of usage. There were no windows, no sources of light aside from a tiny square that could barely be considered a window at all.

Her clothes, once vibrant silks and fine lace, were now reduced to rotten dirty rags. How many others had worn these same threads in the face of their punishments? Her hands, once soft and manicured, were now pale, cracked, and filthy. Her nails, which she used to keep polished to perfection, were jagged and broken, remnants of the life she once led.

She couldn’t remember how long she had been imprisoned. Weeks? Months? Time had lost all meaning. At first, she had tried to keep track, to count the days as they passed, but after a while, it seemed pointless. No one was coming to rescue her. No one would plead for her pardon.

Not that there was anyone left to do so.

Her mind circled back to that awful day, the day everything crumbled beneath her. She had stood, defiant and proud, watching as the royal court passed judgment on her. Her family had stood behind her, unwilling to believe that their beloved daughter, their Rosalind, could be capable of such cruelty. They had been wrong. She knew as much.

She didn’t want them to try and defend her, to make excuses for her actions. There were no excuses. But Rosalind was their daughter. They defended her to the very end.

And because of her, they had paid a terrible price. One by one, they had fallen—her parents, her siblings, and even the servants who she used without mercy. All because of her foolish, selfish actions.

Rosalind squeezed her eyes shut, trying to block out the images of their lifeless bodies, but the memories were burned into her mind. She could still hear the terrified screams of her sister Eliza, could still see the horrified look on her mother’s face, could still feel the cold steel of the prince’s gaze as he condemned her.

No one had come to her aid. No one had tried to stop it. Why would they? She was the villainess, after all. She deserved her punishment.

Rosalind let her head fall back against the stone wall, the cold pressing into her scalp. She had replayed that day in her mind a thousand times, every agonizing moment of it. The blood. The screams. She could still remember how long it took for Eliza to die. That little girl had been so scared, she didn’t want to end up like the others. But in the end she had the same fate. Was this all worth it for revenge? For the sake of some foolish love?

Rosalind’s lips twisted into a bitter, broken smile. No, love had nothing to do with it. She had never truly loved Prince Vicente. She had wanted him. Desired him, yes. But love? She realized now that she hadn’t known love, not really.

The sound of footsteps echoed down the hallway, breaking through her thoughts. Heavy boots against stone. They were coming for her.

The door to her cell creaked open, and a guard stepped inside, his face impassive. He said nothing as he grabbed her arm, yanking her to her feet with more force than necessary. Rosalind didn’t resist. There was no point. She had long since resigned herself to her fate.

As they walked down the dim corridor, Rosalind’s body moved mechanically, as though it no longer belonged to her. Her mind drifted. She had always known it would end this way. It was the nature of stories. The villainess never saw a happy ending.

They reached the doors that led to the stage of her demise, and the guard shoved her forward. Rosalind stumbled but regained her footing just in time to see the blood-streaked wooden planks beneath her feet. The sight of it made her stomach churn. The blood of others—other traitors, criminals, and now her own family—dripped down like red curtains at the end of a play.

Rosalind laughed, though it came out as more of a hoarse, ragged cough. The irony wasn’t lost on her. Here she was, being led to the end, where the villainess faces due justice. And once that justice fell along with the sharp blade, the curtains of the play would fall too.

No one would mourn her. No one would miss her.

The crowd that had gathered was silent, waiting for the inevitable. Rosalind glanced around and recognized many of the faces—nobles who had once bowed to her, servants who had once feared her. Now, they looked at her with disdain, with hatred. Part of her wanted to curse them, to tell them to turn away, but she knew they had every right to watch. She had wronged them all, and now they would see her punished.

But as she was led onto the stage and placed in the brace, as her eyes caught sight of the sharp, glistening blade above her that would end her life, a new sensation washed over her: fear.

She didn’t want to die.

Her breath caught in her throat, her chest tightening with panic. The sight of the blade, hanging menacingly above the platform, sent a chill through her bones. Her legs felt weak, and she stumbled backwards. The man holding her held her tightly in place without mercy.

But it wasn’t just fear that gripped her. It was something else. A deep, painful sadness that twisted in her gut.

She hadn’t wanted this. She had never wanted her family to die. She had never wanted her sister, her sweet Eliza, to suffer. But they had—all because of her. She had prepared herself for this, she was ready to resign herself to the inevitable, but now in the face of it, she hesitated.

She didn’t want to die.

Tears welled up in her eyes as she heard her sentence being read out to the crowd, her knees buckling beneath her. She stared at those watching below and then up at the blade that would soon see her end. For the first time in years, Rosalind felt completely powerless.

"I'm sorry," Rosalind whispered, her voice so soft it was almost lost in the noise of the crowd. “I didn’t want you all to die for me.”

But there was no one there to hear her. No one was there to forgive her, no one was there to comfort her, and no one was there to wipe her tears away.

Rosalind had created her own loneliness.

And she didn’t want to die.

Then again, neither had the people who had died because of her.

She closed her eyes, bracing herself for the end. She had lost everything. She had been a fool. And now, she would pay the price.

The sharp sound of the blade being released cut through the air, and in that final moment, Rosalind’s thoughts drifted not to herself, not even to the prince who had once been her obsession, but to her little sister. Eliza.

“I’m sorry, Eliza,” Rosalind whispered, knowing the words were futile. Eliza wasn’t here. No one was here to hear her apology. It was too late. Too late to make amends.

Even at the end, she couldn’t tell her family how much she had loved them. Perhaps she was scared of facing their judgment. Would they have accepted her love, knowing the terrible things she had done?

Maybe if she had told them, it wouldn’t hurt so much. Perhaps if she had laid her heart bare, if she had been honest with them—honest with herself—the weight of her guilt wouldn’t be this unbearable.

But no, it still hurt. Even at the end, it still hurt.

The blade struck, and the world went dark.

Chapter 2 - Impossibilities

It was impossible.

Rosalind blinked, her heart pounding in her chest as her gaze swept across her surroundings, trying to make sense of the impossible. How could this be? She had felt the cold bite of steel, the weight of guilt crushing her chest as her life slipped away.

And yet… here she was.

She was back in her old bedroom.

Morning light filtered through the windows, casting a warm glow over the familiar room. The ornate canopy bed with its purple drapes, the mahogany vanity where she often prepared herself for battles amongst the other noblewomen, the grand mirror that reflected her undeniable beauty—it was all there, exactly as it had been in the past.

All of the lavender colored ornaments she had collected over the years, but were confiscated… They were there too. She had always loved the color lavender, but now, it all felt alien.

How could this be real?

Rosalind clutched her arms tightly around herself, trying to quell her trembling. Her breaths came in ragged gasps, and her eyes darted around the room frantically, as if expecting it to crumble before her, revealing its illusion.

The cold, damp prison cell, the screams of her family, the sins she had made—those were all real. They had to be, right?

Or… was it all a dream?

No, it couldn’t be, everything was too vivid, too realistic, too…

Rosalind whimpered, the sound slipping from her lips before she could stop it. How could she be here? After everything she had done? This was not what she deserved. It was not fair.

I don’t deserve this!

She had felt the weight of her guilt, and had seen the consequences of her actions. The blood of her sister, her parents, her siblings, her servants—all of it was on her hands.

How could she live on without them, knowing they were gone?

Her breath hitched in her throat as the door to her bedroom creaked open, pulling her from the spiraling thoughts that threatened to overwhelm her.

“My lady, are you awake?”

Rosalind froze.

Her heart pounded in her chest as her gaze snapped to the door. A figure entered, moving with practiced grace. The familiar sound of light footsteps, the gentle white skirt—she had always relied on her maids for her evil deeds—but the sight of this maid made her stomach twist in knots.

She couldn’t stop the shaking that engulfed her body.

Bethany…

The maid who had been with her since childhood, who had followed every order and faced the full brunt of Rosalind’s wrath. Before, she was nothing but a scapegoat, a pawn who could take the fall when needed.

But upon seeing her cold, lifeless body, Rosalind had realized how much Bethany cared about her. Even at her end, Bethany had been on Rosalind’s side.

None of that mattered now, though. She had died along with the other servants, just another unfortunate casualty.

And yet, here she was. Alive. Smiling. Serving tea.

Rosalind couldn’t bring herself to move. She couldn’t breathe.

This can’t be real.

The maid walked to the side of the room and set the tea tray on the table, as if nothing had changed. As if the world around her hadn’t crumbled. As if Rosalind hadn’t destroyed everyone she had known.

“Bethany…” Rosalind whispered, her voice trembling. Her throat tightened painfully as she uttered the word.

Bethany glanced up at her curiously, her brows furrowing in concern at Rosalind’s pale face. “My lady, are you unwell?”

She stepped closer, placing a hand on her forehead. “You don’t seem to have a fever. Shall I fetch the physician? You look as though you’ve seen a ghost.”

A ghost.

Bethany… you’re the ghost… you’re not supposed to be alive.

Rosalind couldn’t take her eyes off her. Every fiber of her body screamed that this was wrong, that this shouldn’t be happening. Her thoughts were spinning, she wanted to scream out, to chastise Bethany, but what had she done wrong?

Was this some sort of cruel punishment? Was this a prison in hell where she would be forced to watch the people she failed living lives that they never could because of her?

Or was this something else?

Rosalind swallowed hard and took a deep breath. Her voice shook as she slowly asked, “Bethany, what year and day is it?”

Bethany blinked, clearly surprised by the odd question, but she answered dutifully, “Why, it’s the 24th of Springtide, my lady. The year is 1432, of course.”

Rosalind held a hand over her mouth in disbelief.

Three years.

She had gone back in time, three years before everything had gone so horribly wrong.

This was real. Somehow, impossibly, she had been given a second chance at life. But how? Why?

Her body trembled violently as the weight of this revelation settled over her like a suffocating blanket. Did she deserve this? Could she fix the pain she had caused? Could she stop herself from succumbing to the terrible envy that once consumed her?

Could she… save her family?

"My lady?" Bethany’s soft voice broke through her thoughts, bringing her back to the present. "The lord and lady are waiting for you in the dining room. Shall I clean you up?"

Rosalind nodded, her hands dropping to her sides. As Bethany dressed her up for breakfast, Rosalind considered the truth of her surroundings. Her fingers clenched into shaky fists.

I can stop it. I can save Eliza.

But was she ready to face them? Could she see the living faces of her family without breaking? She nearly broke when she saw Bethany. What would happen if she saw those closest to her?

Rosalind braced herself as she stood at the doors to the dining room. Behind those doors were the family she had once doomed.

The doors opened slowly, and Rosalind’s breath caught in her throat.

Her parents. Her brothers. And most of all…

“Rose you’re awake!”

Eliza…

Her little sister, staring at her with that warm, comforting smile she had always worn. The smile Rosalind lost at the end of it all.

Tears blurred Rosalind’s vision, spilling over before she could stop them. Eliza was alive. But why did it hurt so much to see her? Why did the sight of her sister, smiling and happy, fill Rosalind with such unbearable sorrow?

“Rosalind, what’s wrong?” Her mother’s concerned voice broke through the haze of emotion.

Rosalind quickly wiped her face. “It’s nothing. I was just… thinking about something.”

She walked over and sat at the table, her nerves still shaken.

As they began to eat, an eerie quiet settled over the dining room. No one knew where the tension was coming from, but Rosalind felt uncomfortable. She couldn’t eat her food with her family next to her. Not when their deaths were so firmly engraved in her brain.

“Rose, are you okay?” Eliza asked, noticing her deathly expression.

“I’m fine,” Rosalind lied.

“Really? But you’re so pale. You look like you’ve seen a ghost,” Rosalind’s mother said.

Rosalind frowned.

There was that word again.

A ghost.

They were all ghosts, even Rosalind. They were all supposed to be dead.

Rosalind shook where she sat, her palpitations clearly visible.

“I’m sorry…” she whispered, tears building in her eyes.

A gentle hand rested on her shoulder, and Rosalind flinched instinctively. Looking up to see Eliza glancing over at her in confusion, she frowned. She didn’t want comfort, especially not from Eliza.  

“Rose, what’s wrong?” Eliza asked, her voice soft and hesitant. “You’re scaring me…”

Rosalind felt her heart twist painfully in her chest. Scaring her? That was nothing compared to everything else she had done.

There was no room for pity. Rosalind simply didn’t want it.

If you want to have any feelings for me, please let it be hate. Don’t show me sympathy. I was a perpetrator, not a victim.

Rosalind clenched her fists, not making eye contact with anyone, despite the fact that everyone was currently staring at her. She couldn’t even bring herself to answer Eliza.

“Rosalind, are you not feeling well?” her brother Felix asked worriedly.

Stop it. Rosalind shook her head.

“Rosalind, if you’re sick, you don’t have to hide it,” her brother Harold said, also worried.

Please stop it! Rosalind’s head sank.

“Rosalind, please answer us. We’re worried about you,” her father said, concerned.

Rosalind had had enough. She was tired of all of the concern, the worried looks, the pitiful words. She didn’t want this, none of it!

Once upon a time, it would have made her happy, but this was not that time. Things had changed far too much.

Rosalind stood from her chair, surprising everyone in the room.

“I… I need some air,” she whispered, her voice barely audible.

Without waiting for a response, she turned on her heel and fled from the room, her heart racing and her mind spinning. She couldn’t stay there, not surrounded by the people she had destroyed.

She barely registered the familiar sights around—the polished marble floors, the ornate paintings, the soft rustle of curtains in the breeze. This didn’t feel like the past. This was a prison of her own memories, of the time she had taken for granted once before.

Finding herself in the garden, Rosalind collapsed onto a nearby bench, her body shaking violently with untold feelings. She buried her face in her hands, trying to calm her racing heart, but it was no use. The tears just wouldn’t stop.

She should have been thankful for this time, for the chance to be with her family again. But the sight of them broke her. It reminded her of the fact that she had destroyed this peace with her foolish actions.

Rosalind knew she had to do something. She couldn’t bring upon the future again. And as it was her who caused all of it, it was only fair that she pay the price.

Rosalind had to lock herself away.

Chapter 3 - Self-Imposed House Arrest

For noble society, parties were considered the most dangerous battlefields of them all.

How one performs at a party could affect their standing in the social sphere, in both good and bad ways. You never wanted to make a fool of yourself. That would destroy you, as no one would ever take you seriously again.

But an absence was more striking than any foolish actions. There were some parties you just couldn’t miss. You wouldn’t want to make enemies by turning certain families down.

Melody Astrale walked through the ballroom of her illustrious manor with practiced elegance. The walls shimmered with the glow of diamonds and crystals, while arches and centerpieces were coated in gold.

Melody herself was adorned with all kinds of shine. She wore diamond earrings, a blue dress lined with crystals, and her baby-blue hair was perfectly clean, to the extent that it sparkled every time she walked.  

Melody loved all things beautiful and shiny. She adored rare crystals and gems the most, acting with any means necessary to increase her expensive collection. Of course, this made her the enemy of many people. Parties were tiring for her, because of this reason. She loved showing off her success, but she couldn’t stand hearing the whispers of those who disliked her.

But on this particular night, Melody felt free. For once in her life, she didn’t hate the idea of attending parties. Why?

Her biggest enemy wasn’t present.

The whispers that spread throughout the crowd weren’t about her. The gossip was on the one person who should have been there, who wouldn’t miss an opportunity to make a fool of greedy Melody.

Rosalind Harcourt was not here.

For two weeks, the woman who had once commanded the attention of every room she entered had disappeared. A mystery had begun to spread throughout the country, growing from hushed whispers over tea to loud speculation in taverns.

Wherever nobles gathered, and even amongst the commoners, one question dominated the conversation:

Where had that terrible villainess gone?

Rosalind had always been the talk of parties, even when she wasn’t present. After all, she was the perfect double-edged sword.

On one hand, she was the ideal noblewoman—refined, intelligent, and perfectly poised when she wished to be. Her beauty was well known throughout the kingdom, and her skill in navigating the noble scene made many envious.

But on the other hand, she was as unpredictable as a sudden storm. One moment, she would dazzle with her charm; the next, she would cut you down with a cruel remark or lash out over the smallest slight.

The worst part was, no one could openly confront her. Not only was she the daughter of a powerful noble family, but she was also the consort to the crown prince.

Who would dare to stand against her?

But now, for the first time in years, Rosalind herself was not the center of discussion. Her absence had become far more interesting to talk about.

It was well known that Rosalind always attended any party she could, because that allowed her to flaunt her power. Sure, she didn’t go to every party, but the trick was that you could never predict where she would show up.

Yet, in this case, she wasn’t making any appearances. Rumors had begun to spread.

The nobles whispered among themselves, exchanging wild stories about why she had locked herself away. Some said she had fallen ill, others believed she had died in secret.

A few even whispered that she was being punished—had she committed some sort of terrible crime? Rosalind was a villainess, so it was no surprise to think she may have done something wrong.

But she had done so many other things practically in broad daylight and gotten away with it.

Her servants knew the kind of terrible schemes she hatched against noblewomen and noblemen who simply got on her nerves. Why would she hide away only now?

Melody, standing among her circle of friends, allowed herself a satisfied smile as she overheard the latest round of rumors circulating the room. It wasn’t often that she could enjoy herself at one of these parties without having to worry about Rosalind’s sharp tongue or calculating gaze.

Tonight, for the first time in what felt like ages, Melody could bask in the attention without fear of being upstaged by her greatest rival.

“Lady Melody,” one of her companions, a fellow noblewoman named Lady Vella, chuckled lightly. “You seem unusually cheerful tonight.”

Melody lips curved into a smug smile. “Why wouldn’t I be? That detestable villainess isn’t here to spoil my mood.”

The women around her shared knowing glances. Rosalind’s absence had been a topic of speculation for days, and every new theory seemed wilder than the last.

“Where do you think she is?” Lady Vella asked, her voice dropping to a conspiratorial whisper. “Her absence is certainly… unnerving, to say the least.”

“I heard,” another noblewoman, Lady Yunee, interjected, “that she’s locked herself away in her estate. My handmaiden is friends with one of her maids, and she said Rosalind has refused to leave her room for weeks. She even sent out a message—something about ‘house arrest.’ What do you think that means?”

Melody’s smile widened. “Isn’t it obvious? The prince has finally tired of her antics and given her the punishment she deserves.”

Gasps followed, the suggestion carrying weight among the gossip-hungry crowd. "The prince?" Lady Vella leaned in, her voice barely a whisper. "What makes you believe that?"

“Well, why else would such a deplorable woman disappear from the spotlight? Clearly she became enemies with the wrong person. Definitely deserved, I say.”

The noblewomen nodded in eager agreement. Lady Rosalind had always been a thorn in their collective sides, a looming presence who thrived on intimidation. But now, with her mysterious absence, they felt confident in speaking their grievances.

"She was always targeting you, wasn’t she?" Lady Vella’s voice held a trace of sympathy, but her eyes sparkled with curiosity. "This must feel incredibly liberating.”

"Liberating," Melody echoed, the word rolling off her tongue. "Yes, I suppose that’s exactly how it feels."

"Understandable. Though I do wonder," Lady Yunee started cautiously, "what exactly did you do to earn Lady Rosalind’s ire?"

Melody thought for a moment and then chuckled again.

“Let’s just say… there was something she possessed that I wanted, and she didn’t particularly like my methods of procuring it.”

"My, how petty of her," Lady Vella remarked, raising an eyebrow. "She despises you over such a trivial matter?"

Melody shook her head. “Well, you never know with villainesses. Sometimes they antagonize you for the sake of it.”

The group laughed, but there was an edge of unease beneath their amusement. As much as they delighted in tearing Rosalind down behind her back, the fear of her return made them hesitate.

What if, one day, she returned with a deep vengeance?

As the conversation continued, Duke Hartwen, an older nobleman with a sharp gaze and a clean-cut appearance, approached the group. His arrival immediately commanded attention.

“Lady Melody,” he greeted her with a respectful nod, “Once again you’ve outdone yourself.”

“Duke Hartwen, it’s always a pleasure to see you at my parties,” Melody replied smoothly, offering her hand for a courteous shake. “Is there something you wish to discuss? I trust everything is to your liking?”

“Of course, of course. I just happened to overhear your conversation. I presume you all were discussing the ‘missing villainess’ and the rumors surrounding her?”

“Indeed. Her disappearance has caused much confusion.”

Duke Hartwen nodded. “It’s strange,” he mused, swirling his glass of wine thoughtfully. “Her parents don’t seem to know anything either. You’d think they’d be involved somehow, but they are just as confused as we are.”

Melody pondered. “Well… we’ve all heard the whispers, haven’t we? Apparently, she hasn’t requested any visitors. Not her parents, not even the prince. And she hasn’t demanded her usual luxuries.”

Duke Hartwen raised an eyebrow. “No visitors? None at all? That’s unusual. Lady Rosalind thrives on attention.”

“Exactly,” Melody agreed. “Which is why something doesn’t add up. If she were ill, surely the court physicians would have been summoned. And if she were being punished, the rumors would have reached us by now. But it’s all quiet. Too quiet.”

Duke Hartwen sipped his wine, his mind churning. “Whatever the reason, it’s only a matter of time before she returns. And when she does, I’m sure she will never hear the end of it.”

Melody crossed her arms, her jaw tightening. “I think it would be better if she never returned at all.”

Bethany knocked gently on the heavy oak door of Rosalind’s bedroom.

"My lady, I’ve brought you some food."

There was no response. Bethany waited a moment longer, her frown deepening as the silence stretched on. It had been days since Rosalind had willingly spoken to anyone, and even longer since she’d emerged from her room.

“My lady…” Bethany's voice softened with concern as she tried again, but there was still no reply. With a resigned sigh, she placed the tray of food gently on the floor, careful not to make too much noise, and turned to leave.

As Bethany’s footsteps faded down the corridor, slowly the door opened. Rosalind peeked out only to grab her food and then closed it once again, locking it tightly.

She walked slowly across the dimly lit room, her bare feet brushing over the cold floor. She made her way to the wide window that overlooked the estate's gardens. Once upon a time, she would look out this window with a sense of pride, but now she only felt crushing pain seeing her estate.

She had lost this place once before, because of her failures. And she had many, many failures.

Rosalind sat down on the floor and rested against the window, taking small bites of her food.

Right now, she looked like a ghost. Her skin was pale, her entire form was thin, and she had large bags under her eyes. It was like she was back in that old dirty prison cell again.

Except, this prison was one of her own making.

Rosalind simply couldn’t sleep. She kept having nightmares of the past, in which the consequences of her actions played out before her on repeat. Every morning, she would wake up trembling and drenched in sweat. Desperately, she would wipe her hands on the bed sheets, as if cleaning off blood that wasn’t there.

The skin on her hands had grown red and bruised from the constant rubbing, but no amount of scrubbing could erase her sins. Guilt was overtaking her to no end.

More than once, Rosalind had stood at the edge of her window, staring down at the concrete below, her mind tempted by the thought of ending it all.

But death still scared her, even now.

No, it wasn’t because she felt an obligation to apologize or repent, though that was part of the reason. It was because she was afraid.

Rosalind placed her half-eaten food aside and lay down on the floor, her body curling into the fetal position as she gazed blankly out the window.

“I want to forget… let me forget…”

She muttered, her voice soft and weak.

Chapter 4 - Nightmares In The Greenhouse

Evening rain battered against the windows of Rosalind’s bedroom. The sharp patter was a familiar sound by now; it was if the sky itself was following her solemnity.  

It was the fourth night in a row she couldn’t sleep. Or was it the fifth? Rosalind had lost track of the sleepless nights, the anxiety-filled hours spent lying awake as terrible memories, guilt-ridden feelings, and sadness clawed at her mind.

Sleep was no better. Everytime she closed her eyes, she found herself transported back to that prison cell, watching everyone she loved suffer and die, waiting for her own inevitable end. She remembered the damp air, the awful smell of blood, and the cold metal shackles that bound her wrists.

Living with guilt was miserable, but it was far better than reliving that nightmare.

Hearing the shutters rattle, Rosalind opened her window. A cold gust and rain struck her face, but she didn’t flinch at the elements. Her gaze drifted to the dark horizon, where a small glass structure lay distant, but visible.

A look of recognition filled her eyes, and before she knew it, she’d thrown on her cloak and slipped quietly through the silent halls of the manor. This was a spur of the moment decision, but one that Rosalind desired.

She had been in her room, engrossed in her turbulent thoughts, and held the guilt of her past, for far too long. She still felt somewhat guilty, though; clearly everyone was worried about her, even the servants were wondering if something had happened.

She knew she couldn’t let this go on.

But part of her was thankful for this long period of solitude. Without it, she probably wouldn’t be thinking about all of these things. She would have never realized how much she missed out on.

The rain slowed to a light drizzle as Rosalind stepped outside into the night air. Storm clouds still dotted the sky, leaving the world in a palpable darkness, darker than even the night sky could provide.

Rosalind pulled her cloak tighter around herself. Right now, she looked miserable, like a vagrant or a hermit. Her clothes were becoming dirtier, but she was no stranger to it.

It was wonderful to even have comfortable clothes at all, when she considered the conditions of the prison cell she was once confined to.

The long troughs of her family’s extensive gardens came into view as she walked along the dark pathways. This late at night, everywhere felt like a maze to traverse.

Eventually, the faint outline of a glass building grew clearer and clearer as she approached. It had been many years since Rosalind visited the greenhouse that lay on the Harcourt manor grounds.

She was just a little girl at that time, an innocent dove that knew nothing of the world she was growing up in. She simply loved to play with her friends and wanted nothing more than to care for the lavender in her greenhouse.

But the truth of life is more complex than that. Far more complex.

Rosalind felt a heavy sense of guilt as she observed the greenhouse’s condition. It had been built for her, a present to celebrate her birth, but once she became an adult, she paid it no mind.

There wasn’t time for flowers and lavender, she reasoned, as she had to become an adult worthy of her family name. She had to become someone the prince would love.

Love…

Rosalind cursed the word love. At least, the love for another, the feeling of desire and lust.

No, she preferred childhood love, when she could love anything, anyone, when the things she loved most were the lavender petals of flowers.

Rosalind grasped an aging handle and pushed open the greenhouse door, which resisted under the tangle of vines that now claimed it. A blast of earthly air greeted her, filling her with a sense of nostalgia.

“It’s been a while,” she muttered with a small smile.

As she gingerly traveled the familiar paths between the plant beds, she experienced bursts of recognition at various sights.

She remembered the rare species, the vines and exotic roots that had been sourced from far away places. Most of all she remembered her lavender, which was still preserved as it had been so many years ago.

She ran her hands through the familiar purple petals and smiled. These flowers had been her closest friends, she had confided many childish whims to them. But now those were all in the past.

Rosalind sighed. How many times had she experienced this overwhelming sadness?

She stared at her reflection in one of the glass windows. In the dim light, her reflection looked pale and ghostly. Her once strong gaze was now dull and lifeless.

The sight ate at her pride. Rosalind had always tried so hard to appear strong and confident. She never wanted to show weakness, because weakness was the biggest weapon of a noble. She knew the only way she could live up to her family name was by erasing all signs of weakness.

This greenhouse was one of the casualties. She had chosen to forget its existence because it was a weakness. A strong family heir shouldn’t concern themselves with flowers.

But now, standing there in the middle of this place she once loved, she hated herself for doing that. How could she allow it to fall into such disarray?

Rosalind reached out and touched the dirty glass. A chill ran down her spine as she stared at her reflection, her other self seeming to look back with cold, heartless judgment. She could almost hear words of mocking, echoes of her own condemnation.

You don’t deserve this second chance.

You’ll only hurt them again.

You’re a monster, Rosalind.

Who would ever forgive you?

She squeezed her eyes shut, trying to drown the voices out as they grew louder. The guilt was suffocating, pressing down on her like an unmovable weight.

“No,” she whispered, her voice trembling. “I’m not that person anymore. I can repent for my sins.”

But the words sounded unbelievable, even to her own ears.

Rosalind stared up at the glass ceiling of the greenhouse and sighed. As the rain trickled down outside, she felt so hopeless.

At this point, she would take anything, anything, to rid herself of the awful past that just wouldn’t leave her alone.

A sudden creak from the greenhouse door pulled her from her thoughts. Rosalind froze, her eyes snapping open. In the doorway stood a figure, her form encased in shadow, but unmistakable.

“Eliza…?” Rosalind whispered, her voice barely a breath.

The girl took a step forward, her face hidden, obscured, but her eyes… her eyes held a gaze that seemed to pierce directly into Rosalind’s soul.

“You let me die, Rose.” Eliza’s voice was soft, yet forceful enough to send shivers down her spine.

Rosalind stumbled back, a hoarse gasp escaping her lips. “N-no… I… I didn’t mean to—”

“I always looked up to you,” Eliza ignored her pleas, her voice icy with accusation. “But in the end, you really are a villainess. I never wanted to believe it.”

Rosalind shook her head, holding her hands against her ears as if she could block out the poisonous words.

“No… stop it!” she cried, her voice trembling. “I tried to protect you! I never wanted you to suffer because of me!”

Eliza mechanically tilted her head. “Then why did you let it happen?” she hissed, taking a step closer. “When I begged for them to stop, did you listen, Rose? Did you protect me? Or did you give up when we all needed you the most?”

Rosalind’s vision blurred with tears. Memories of that terrible day flashed before her, the blood, the cries, the desperate pleas. She fell to her knees and whimpered.

“Please,” she whispered, the words barely audible. “No more…”

“This is your eternal punishment, Rose.” Every bit of Eliza’s words struck her. “You should suffer for everything.”

“Eliza…” Rosalind choked, her gaze filled with unknowable despair. “I’m sorry. I am so, so sorry…”

The figure inched closer, a twisted smile spreading across her face. She pulled Rosalind into a hug, and for a second, she had a foolish hope that Eliza would forgive her. But instead she leaned into Rosalind’s ear and whispered: “I’ll never forgive you.”

Rosalind shook in the cold, uncaring embrace. She tried to push Eliza away, but Eliza wasn’t letting her go. “No—”

Shadows spilled from Eliza’s form, overtaking Rosalind with an impenetrable darkness. “I hate you,” came Eliza’s final whisper, a vow that echoed in the void.  

In that darkness, hearing those awful words from the sister she loved so much, Rosalind couldn’t help but yell out: “STOP IT!”

The air went silent and the darkness faded. She realized she hadn’t moved from where she was standing once before. And the greenhouse door was still closed.

Eliza wasn’t there. Of course she wasn’t, it was too late for her to be awake.

It was simply a manifestation of her own inner hatred.

Eliza would never think those things. She would never say those things. Eliza had loved her, trusted her, depended on her.

But Rosalind had been telling herself these things all along. And she had broken that love and trust when her little sister needed it the most.

She couldn’t let this go on.

How long would the torment continue if she allowed it?

Rosalind’s heart ached as she stood in the palpable silence. She had been given this second chance, this opportunity to rewrite her mistakes.

But could she prevent herself from repeating those past actions? What if she led everyone to ruin in another way? What if it was all futile?

Then a distant memory surfaced: “Rose!” Eliza’s warm voice, her cheerful laughter, and her wonderful smile. It was only a memory.

But Rosalind had a realization.

She didn’t know whether it was possible to truly repent for everything she had done. But regardless of that fact, she owed it to her little sister to make amends.

If she could only retain that smile, that lovely little bit of affection that kept her from going over the edge so many times in the past. That smile which she turned away out of fear of weakness.

But not only that, she owed it to everyone who suffered for her mistakes in her first life. She had to make it up to her parents, the servants, to Bethany, who served her so dutifully…

Rosalind looked around the greenhouse, her gaze settling on the lavender. She picked a small bundle, clutching the flowers like a lifeline. Then she turned and stepped out into the fresh air.

The storm had passed, and the clouds had parted, revealing a clear, starlit sky. She took a deep breath and allowed the cool night to engulf her.

She couldn’t let this go on.

Rosalind had whispered those words to herself enough times, and now, it was truly time to end the charade of ‘house arrest.’

She didn’t want to face judgment—not again. But deep down, she also knew that judgment hurt far more when faced alone.

Rosalind had only one choice. If she wanted to defeat her inner judgment, all she had to do was replace it with repentance. Endless, painful, unrelenting repentance.

Chapter 5 - First Steps Of Repentance

In the early morning, Eliza approached Rosalind’s door. It had been a while since she had seen any sign of her older sister. Rosalind had withdrawn so completely into her solitude, that Eliza wondered if something happened to her.

Standing there, she was tempted to knock, but she knew Rosalind wanted to be alone. She had turned everyone else away, why would this time be any different?

Eliza had never seen this side of her sister before. Rosalind had always been such an untouchable figure, poised and perfect, someone she looked up to. And yet, Eliza sensed that her sister had become fragile, solemn, and resigned. It honestly scared her.

As she turned to leave, she heard the door creak softly. Rosalind stood there, still dressed in her night clothes, her face pale but calm. Her expression was sadder than Eliza had ever seen, and her eyes held an unfamiliar warmth.

“Rose?” Eliza whispered, not daring to believe this was truly her proud sister.

Rosalind smiled—a small, genuine smile that made her heart leap. “Good morning, Eliza.”

Her voice was full of warmth and gentleness—it was the same voice Eliza remembered from her childhood, when her older sister would read her bedtime stories.

“Are… are you still sad?” Eliza asked, hesitant and uncertain. She’d never known how to talk with Rosalind as she got older and more refined. It always seemed like her sister was far out of reach.

A faint shadow passed over Rosalind’s face as her gaze shifted momentarily to the floor. “A little,” she admitted quietly. “But now I have work to do.”

“Work?” Eliza asked curiously, tilting her head. Was she going to lose her sister again? After showing this genuine side to herself, was she really going to go back to being stoic and cold?

Before Eliza could respond, Rosalind leaned in and pulled her into a warm, almost desperate embrace. Her eyes went wide. It had been years since her sister hugged her like this, her touch full of love and affection.

“As my dearest little sister, I love you, Eliza. And I’m sorry for not admitting this sooner,” Rosalind whispered.

Eliza’s eyes widened as she returned the embrace, her mind reeling. Her sister had always been so distant, never showing affection at any moment. She simply didn’t know what to make of this soft, earnest side of Rosalind.

A thousand questions filled her mind, but only one spilled out. “What… happened?”

Rosalind gave a small, sad smile as she pulled back. “I just… realized a lot of things,” she murmured. “And now I want to make things right.”

Her words were strange and mysterious, but Eliza found herself nodding. Rosalind had clearly changed—there was no denying it.

“Now, can you fetch Bethany for me? I have something to tell her.”

Eliza nodded silently, trying to mask her confusion as she hurried off down the hall to find Bethany. She had no idea what happened to her sister, nor did she really understand her actions.

What could affect such a strong person to this extent?

Rosalind watched her little sister leave the room, her heart swelling with bittersweet emotion. Settling into a nearby chair, she took a steadying breath.

She knew her actions might have seemed strange to Eliza, who had known her as guarded and cold. But Rosalind couldn’t go back to being that person, not now, not ever. Her former life had told her what consequences would follow if she allowed herself to fall again.

The memory of that vision in the greenhouse—a nightmare, yet so vivid it felt real—still lingered in her mind. Every bitter word and hurtful accusation seared her soul, leaving mental wounds that she couldn’t erase. She still heard the terrible words that hurt her the most.

You’re just a villainess.

The words tore at her, a relentless reminder of who she had once been, the mistakes she had made, the lives she had ruined.

Rosalind was scared.

But she couldn’t let herself fall to that fear. At the very least, she had to overcome it for the sake of those she hurt, so she could apologize to them.

Her thoughts were interrupted by a soft knock. Turning, she saw Bethany, her handmaid, standing in the doorway. Bethany’s face held a mixture of surprise and worry as she took in Rosalind’s weary expression and her disheveled attire.

“You called for me, my lady?” Bethany asked, her tone respectful but tinged with unease.

“Yes, Bethany,” Rosalind said, her heart pounding nervously. She motioned to the chair next to her. “Sit with me.”

Bethany did as she was told, but her gaze lingered on a small vase of lavender flowers arranged on the nearby side table, presumably from the previous night. They had been perfectly watered and cared for.

How long had it been since she saw those flowers being displayed so openly? She was under the impression that Rosalind had become ashamed of her gardening past, but now this?

“My lady?” Bethany asked, her eyes shifting back to Rosalind, whose gaze softened.

“Oh, I noticed you looking at those,” Rosalind said, her voice quiet. She picked up the vase and looked at the flowers with a faint smile. “I was foolish to give this up,” she murmured. “How could I have forgotten my own roots?”

Bethany’s eyes widened. The gentle way Rosalind held the flowers struck her as a new side to her mistress—one she hadn’t seen in quite some time.

Rosalind turned to Bethany. “So Bethany, I would like you to start fetching me some lavender every day for my room. Is that okay?”

“Of course, my lady.” Bethany said with a smile.

Rosalind observed her for a few moments, then looked down, her expression clouding with unease. “There’s something else I wanted to tell you…” she whispered nervously. “It’s hard for me to say.”

Bethany waited, surprised by the rare vulnerability in Rosalind’s tone. “Go ahead, my lady. I’m listening.”

“Bethany, I… I want to apologize to you.” Rosalind began, her voice quiet. “For everything I’ve done. For all the times I’ve used you. Serving my selfishness was truly a fate you didn’t deserve.”

Bethany was left speechless. Rosalind was apologizing to her? The one who always asked her to do nearly impossible things and get her hands dirty for evil schemes?

Surely this couldn’t be real?

“I understand if you find it hard to forgive me,” Rosalind continued sadly. “I would feel the same way.”

A long silence settled between them, and for a moment, Rosalind feared Bethany wouldn’t believe her. But to her surprise, Bethany’s face softened with emotion.

“My lady,” she whispered, her voice soft. “I… I never expected to hear those words from you.”

Rosalind’s own eyes stung as she gave a small, trembling smile. “Then I suppose it’s about time.”

Bethany nodded, her expression one of tentative hope. “If you’re truly set on making amends, my lady, then I will gladly stand by your side.”

Rosalind eyed her in surprise, she could feel her hands shaking, in fact they had been shaking the entire time. This was too fast, something about her acceptance was almost… scary.

“Bethany…” she started, her voice shaking. “You don’t have to lie to me. If you’re upset with what I’ve done and want to leave, you can tell me.”

Bethany placed a hand on Rosalind’s own, and smiled. “I live to serve you, my lady. There have been times when I’ve felt apprehensive, but I would never choose to leave your side. I would take your hand even if you told me not to.”

Rosalind frowned. I know you would. That’s what I’m scared of.

She knew Bethany wasn’t lying. She had seen it first hand, when everything had ended, and everyone lost their lives because of her. Bethany had truly been with her to the very end.

Rosalind steadied herself. She didn’t want tears to start falling. “I want to talk with all of the servants,” she said quietly. “Can you help me apologize to them?”

Bethany smiled. “I would love nothing more, my lady.”

With that, Rosalind’s first true steps towards repentance began. The servants were the first to see just how determined she was to apologize and make amends. She apologized and spoke to everyone—the maids, cooks, butlers, stable hands, and even the groundskeepers.

Through each of them, she learned of their problems, and one by one she tried to help them out. She even helped the gardeners tend to the grounds, and assisted in the kitchen once or twice.

Those with sick loved ones or serious illnesses were given expensive medicine, extra meals were sent home with those struggling, and some were given well-deserved time off.

Her efforts were paying off little by little, until everyone on the estate was whispering about the new Rosalind, and how she had changed for the better.

But Rosalind couldn’t avoid the public eye for too much longer. An event she dreaded was going to begin soon, and it was mandatory for all nobles (if you didn’t want to be ridiculed).

One afternoon, as Rosalind collapsed into a chair, thoroughly exhausted from assisting in the kitchens, Bethany entered the room, holding an ornate envelope. As soon as she saw it, Rosalind groaned.

“My lady, you’ve received an invitation,” Bethany announced, handing her the envelope.

Rosalind took it with a weary sigh and groaned again when she saw where it was from. “So Prince Vicente’s birthday party is approaching…”

“I heard it’s going to be quite grand,” Bethany said, nodding. “Shall we begin preparing dresses?”

Rosalind hated it, but she reluctantly nodded. “Yes, unfortunately.”

Bethany chuckled. “My lady, you’ve become so honest lately. I prefer this side of you.”

Rosalind smiled. “So do I.”

As they looked at the dresses in Rosalind’s closet, Rosalind thought about her plans for the upcoming party. As much as she really didn’t want to go, she also knew this was the perfect opportunity to make amends with the prince. Most importantly, she would also be able to talk with Marciella and apologize to her.

Slowly but surely, Rosalind would begin to heal the wounds she had created in the wake of her foolishness. And honestly, she was looking forward to it.

Chapter 6 - Breaking An Unrequited Love (Part One)

The day Rosalind dreaded the most. Prince Vicente’s birthday party was set to begin on this night.

Rosalind sat patiently as her styling maids decorated her. They had always been experts at this, even in the past, but she had never taken the time to thank them. In fact, she never even considered them. What a mistake.

One of the maids presented her with a wooden box filled with an assortment of beautiful necklaces, their gemstones sparkling under the room’s soft candlelight.

“Which of these would you like to wear, my lady?”

Rosalind gave the pieces a cursory glance and then smiled.

“What about this one?” she suggested, holding up a simple chain necklace that contained a single small crystal in the middle. It was a far cry from the lavish things she usually wore.

The maids exchanged glances, their surprise apparent.

“Are you certain, my lady?” one of them asked. “It’s quite simple compared to what you usually wear.”

“Of course,” Rosalind nodded. “I want to go for a simple look tonight. That’s why I chose this reserved dress, after all.”

The maids looked at each other, then back at Rosalind, and shrugged. “As you say, my lady.” One of them pointed to the bracelet on Rosalind’s wrist, a piece she had long forgotten, but wore proudly as a reminder of her past life, where it all ended. “And your bracelet, my lady? Will you keep it on?”

“Yes.” Rosalind ran her fingers over its aging band, smiling fondly. “It is important to me.”

The maids nodded and began their work.

They worked their magic, using the simple pieces to transform Rosalind into a beauty. Even a reserved attire looked nice on her. For the first time, she felt like she was presenting herself as she truly was, without the need for pretense or show.

When they finally finished, they allowed her to look in the mirror.

“What do you think, my lady?”

Rosalind observed her reflection. Her dress was perfectly subtle and her use of jewelry was excellently minimal. Her hair had also been expertly styled. She smiled.

“I look perfect,” she said genuinely. “Thank you both for always doing your absolute best.”

The maids were in disbelief at her words of appreciation. They had never heard her say such things before, but they could tell by the look in Rosalind’s face that she was being honest. Their expressions warmed in response.

“Thank you, my lady,” they both said, curtsying with shy smiles.

As Rosalind stood to leave, she took one last look and steeled herself. She felt different, not only in appearance, but in her emotions as well. She was going into the battlefield with a lot of preparation, but the outcome truly lay in her actions. She had to make this a night of change and proper amends.

Rosalind left the room and walked along the halls of the manor. Her family was all there in the foyer, waiting for her presence. She smiled upon seeing her little sister Eliza in a cute flower dress, and descended the stairs, pulling her into a loving hug.

“You look so cute, Eliza,” Rosalind said happily.

Eliza was caught off-guard by the sudden affection, but melted into the feeling with a gleeful smile. “And you look beautiful, Rose!”

As everyone became aware of her presence, gasps echoed through the room, everyone’s eyes widening at the sight of her. Her father looked at her with both surprise and approval, while her mother’s brows furrowed at the subtlety she displayed.

“Rosalind… your appearance is so… understated,” one of her brothers said, breaking the silence.

“But she’s still beautiful despite that!” Eliza snapped back with a pout.

“Of course she is,” her father said, “But why so reserved, Rosalind?”

Rosalind offered a small smile, shrugging lightly. “I thought it was time for a change.”

“Entering the Harcourt family!”

The doors to the castle ballroom opened grandly, as whispers filled the air at Rosalind’s presence. It had been a while since anyone saw her. But here she was, and… she seemed different?

Rosalind had always been known for her lavish dresses, but today she was dressed in the most subtle way anyone had ever seen her. On top of that, she carried a calm, almost demure cadence.

“Is that really Lady Rosalind?”

“She’s so muted today. Do you think she’s ill?”

“Look at how close she is with her little sister. Have you ever seen her showing such affection before?”

“What evil scheme is she planning this time?”

Normally, Rosalind would respond to these whispers with strong glares, but today she ignored them completely. She turned to Eliza who was standing by her side.

“Eliza, go with mother and father. You’ll be safer with them,” she whispered. “Mother, that's okay with you, right?”

“Yes, of course it is,” her mother responded with a nod.

“I’m sorry, Eliza,” Rosalind said sadly. “But there are too many bad actors here. I don’t want you to be involved with them.”

Eliza frowned. “Alright.”

Rosalind smiled. “That’s my duty as her older sister.”

“Well, well, well,” said a voice from behind her that she recognized as Melody’s. “Look who’s finally returned.”

Before Melody could respond, Rosalind placed a single lavender flower in her hair. She then walked away. Part of her also wanted to talk with Melody more, to rekindle their friendship, but that would have to wait.

First she had to turn her attention to the prince.

Rosalind went to a nearby table and grabbed a glass of wine. She downed the entire thing, as the doors to the ballroom opened.

“Entering Prince Vicente and Lady Marciella Watrese!”

After all, the consequences of that action were plenty clear to her. So, as they passed by her, rather than anger, Rosalind simply bowed respectfully. It was clearly a surprising gesture, if the loud whispers were anything to go by.

For several long minutes, Rosalind only watched the prince and Marciella interact with other party guests. She didn’t know what feelings she was staring with. Perhaps she was simply letting resignation overtake her, so she could finally move on. Or maybe she still felt a sliver of affection for Prince Vicente. Whatever it was—sadness, resignation, anger—Rosalind didn’t say a single word.

Of course, it wouldn’t be long before people approached her in an attempt to change that. A malicious group of noblewomen walked up to her from behind.

“Lady Rosalind, you’ve returned to high society,” said their leader, sneering, “I suppose that long absence cleansed your mind of foolishness?”

A vein nearly popped in Rosalind’s head, but she controlled herself. She knew that if she wanted to convince everyone of her intent to change, she couldn’t keep retorting to those who provoked her. After all, these people only now had the confidence to criticize her because they presumed she had faced a punishment that humbled her.

To be fair, were they really wrong? Being in solitude for that long did allow her to think about her mistakes.

“You’re absolutely correct, Lady Jeau,” Rosalind nodded thoughtfully. “I have been pretty foolish. If I hurt you in any way, I apologize.”

The other women exchanged confused glances, clearly taken aback by her response. Was this really the villainess Rosalind? They expected some fiery retort or snide remark, but this… she actually offered an apology?

“Lady Rosalind, do you really mean that?” one of the noblewomen asked, her eyes full of doubt. “Or are you scheming something?”

“Of course I mean it,” Rosalind said genuinely. “I don’t intend to cause any disturbances. I’m just enjoying my own company.”

“Really? Surely you must feel some bitterness?” said Lady Jeau, attempting to provoke her once again. “After all, Prince Vincente is getting engaged to Lady Marciella instead of you, his fiance.”

Rosalind's gaze softened as she looked towards Vicente and Marciella. “I was upset once, furious even. But now I just want them to be happy. There never was any place for me.”

As the party continued into the night, Rosalind tried her hardest to reach Prince Vicente. She waited patiently for any opportunity, hoping he would be alone, preferably not near anyone who would immediately start gossiping.

But there was just one problem. Every time Rosalind tried to approach him, something or someone got in the way.

First, a famous duke appeared from nowhere, launching into an animated discussion with the prince about trade and politics. Rosalind lingered, hoping the conversation would end, but it seemed like it never would. And just when she was beginning to fall asleep listening to them talk, it was over.

But as soon as it ended, an eager noblewoman approached him from thin air to try to get his attention with flattery and feigned kindness. Her conversation with him never seemed to end either.

Worst still, even Rosalind’s own nerves were working against her. A rare free moment would seem like a daunting task, and then she was back to square one.

In the end, Rosalind was left with no progress on her attempts to apologize. She never thought it would be so difficult to simply say ‘sorry.’ And she really, really wanted to.

Standing outside on a secluded balcony, Rosalind took a sip of wine and sighed.

“No, no, nothing like that!” Rosalind quickly interjected, shaking her head as a rosy blush grew on her cheeks. “It’s not the poison. I’m fine. Truly.”

Eliza relaxed slightly, though her suspicion lingered. “Then what is it?” she pressed, leaning closer. “There’s another reason, isn’t there?”

Rosalind hesitated, her hand instinctively moving to rest over her stomach. A soft, almost shy smile spread across her face as she met her sister’s gaze. “Yes,” she admitted, her voice quiet. “There is another reason.”

Eliza tilted her head in curiosity, her brow furrowing. Then, a sudden realization struck. Her eyes went wide, and her mouth fell open. “Wait, you don’t mean—?”

Rosalind’s smile grew, her fingers gently tracing the fabric of her dress. “Francis has given me my greatest miracle,” she said, her voice soft.

For a moment, Eliza was silent in shock, processing the revelation. Then, with a delightful squeal, she sprung from her chair and captured Rosalind in a tight hug. “Rose, I’m so happy for you!” she cried, her voice thick with emotion. “So happy!”

Rosalind held her close, overcome with a deep sense of happiness. How long had it been since she was able to relax and enjoy herself like this?

Eliza clung to her, her face buried against Rosalind’s shoulder. Quiet sobs escaped her lips, muffled but full of feeling. “You’re going to be a mother,” she murmured through tears, her voice trembling with delight. “This is wonderful.”

Rosalind laughed softly, rubbing her sister’s hair with affection. “Goodness, Eliza,” she said, struggling to fight back her own tears. “You’re crying more than I am.”

She glanced down, her gaze settling where her hands rested protectively over her stomach. Her smile widened, full of gratitude. Francis had already given her one miracle before, one that had saved her from despair. But now, he had given her another—one greater than she could have ever imagined.

And thankfully, this miracle was hers to nurture in peace.
