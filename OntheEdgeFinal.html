On the Edge

A story written by Lumi.

Premise

Saburo has been dating his good friend Emiko since middle school, but they’re distant with each other. Emiko often comes up with excuses whenever Saburo wants to hang out with her. Popular girl Ayane is dating the popular Naoto, but their relationship is rough. After discovering that Emiko is in a secret relationship with Naoto, Saburo runs away and finds himself on the school rooftop. There, he sees Ayane on the edge. She discovered their secret relationship as well, and is upset. Her entire life is full of misery and she has very few real friends (the ones she has are just taking advantage of her popularity). Saburo stops her from taking her life, and they form a friendship. As they grow closer in their shared sadness, Saburo constantly has to stop Ayane from making a terrible decision. But one day, Ayane greets Saburo on the rooftop with a small smile and invites him to lunch. From there, their new defiant secret relationship begins…

Tags:

Depression, Slow Romance, Hurt/Comfort, Cheating, Broken Friendships, Hidden Personalities, Self Harm, Drama, Mature

Preface

This story contains themes of depression, inferiority, and self-harm. These are heavy topics that should be considered before reading.

PART TWO: ONE STEP FORWARD

Chapter 1 - Flickering Lights

Flickering lights.

Saburo Fujimoto stood at the bottom of the stairwell, gripping the strap of his school bag so tight his knuckles ached.

He knew his relationship with Emiko hadn’t been what it once was. Sure, they were great friends in middle school, but something changed. Or maybe it would be more accurate to say someone.

At the same time, he figured it wasn’t as bad as he thought. They were just going through separate lives, the kind of difficulties everyone faces in long term relationships.

Until he saw her kissing another guy.

He couldn’t get the sight out of his mind. The flickering lights, the rapid speed of the subway as it sped past them, the looks of love on their faces…

How long had it been since Emiko looked at him that way?

He wanted to believe it was a mistake. Maybe she had tripped and Naoto had caught her. Maybe they were talking about something innocent, and Saburo had seen it wrong—

But he definitely hadn't.

He was there, watching every painful second, as Naoto leaned down, brushing his lips against hers. It had been soft, practiced—not the first time.

Saburo’s stomach twisted, almost vomiting in the face of it all. With his heart cracked in two, he forced himself to climb the stairs.

The hallway was empty. Lunch break was almost over. On a typical day, before all of this happened—Saburo didn’t even know when the infidelity started—he would have been sitting across from Emiko like always, enjoying simply being by her side.

Of course, at some point he started pretending he didn’t notice how little she looked at him anymore. Or how he couldn’t feel the widening gap between them with each passing second.

It was just brief discomfort, such feelings would fade with time as they considered the future, right? Perhaps she would look towards him again if he gave her space.

Saburo chuckled sourly. He had been a fool after all. If he had only realized things sooner, he could have prevented his own hurt.

His feet carried him higher. He was at the door to the roof now, for reasons he didn’t quite know. Was it to contemplate in peace? Or was he feeling like ending it all?

Maybe not the latter. Saburo was too much of a coward to do something like that. He had always been. Emiko often reminded him of that fact.

But Saburo wasn’t really thinking anymore. In that cafeteria, surrounded by countless students, sitting directly across from the one person he thought was his best friend, it was pure hell. He needed to be free of it.

He didn't even flinch as he pushed open the heavy metal door at the top of the stairwell and felt the cold wind slap his face.

The rooftop.

It was off-limits, but the lock was broken. Nobody ever came up here.

At least, that was how it was supposed to be.

Saburo froze halfway through the doorway.

Someone was already there.

A girl stood at the very edge of the rooftop, her back to him.

Ayane Shirasaki.

He knew her name—it was impossible not to. Everyone knew her name.

Ayane was the kind of girl who belonged on magazine covers or at the center of conversation. Long, glossy black hair cascaded down her perfectly fit uniform. Black tights stretched over long legs.

She was beautiful, but in a deceptively delicate way. There was something sharp about her—like she could cut you just by looking at you. No one dared insult her to her face.

She stood perfectly still, arms loose at her sides.

The ends of her shoes balanced dangerously on the edge.

Saburo’s breath caught.

He knew what he was seeing.

He knew exactly what she was about to do.

For a brief second, he felt envious. Ayane wasn’t like him, she wasn’t afraid of such a thing.

He should respect her bravery and leave. He shouldn’t get involved. This wasn’t his business.

Then a part of him asked a question he didn’t want to ask: why was she doing it?

Ayane had everything; she was popular, attractive, and extremely loaded. Yet here she was, teetering before the end of it all.

A far part of his brain told him to walk away. But his mouth opened before he could stop himself.

“Don’t.”

Ayane flinched.

For a second, she didn’t turn around. Saburo’s heart slammed against his chest.

Then—slowly—she glanced over her shoulder, eyes narrowed.

“Why not?” Her voice was soft, flat. Not what he expected at all.

Saburo blinked.

How could he answer her? He wasn’t sure why he even said anything in the first place.

Why did stop her? Was he being heroic? Or was it a selfish attempt at currying favor with the most popular girl in school?

“I… I can see what you’re doing,” Saburo said finally, even though she obviously didn’t need him to tell her that.

Ayane's lips curved, but there was no warmth in it.

“Oh?” She tilted her head and gave him a teasing little smile. “What does it look like then?”

Saburo's throat tightened.

He should walk away. He barely knew this girl. If he stopped her now, she'd probably just call him a creep and tell him to mind his own business.

But he couldn't move.

Instead, he took a shaky step forward.

“You’re…” He frowned, unable to say it. “You just shouldn't be up here.”

Ayane's smile flickered.

“And you should?”

He didn't have an answer to that.

The wind howled between them, catching the edge of Ayane's skirt. Saburo's eyes flicked back to her shoes—just a few centimeters from the edge. She was far too close…

“Please…” The word came out smaller than he'd meant. He didn’t even know why he was trying so hard in the first place. “Don't.”

For a moment, Ayane just stared at him.

"Why not?" She repeated, her voice painfully hollow. “Tell me.”

Saburo's heart raced.

Countless meaningless phrases fluttered through his mind.

“Because someone will miss you.”

Would someone miss her? What if he hurt her without wanting to?

“Because you don't deserve this.”

What did he even know about her? They hadn’t even properly met until that very moment.

“Because I don't want to watch anyone else disappear.”

Saburo didn’t even know if he was that noble of a person. Those words were just as hollow as the expression on her face.

In the end, none of those words would come out.

Instead, he blurted the only thing that did:

“Because…. I don't want to be the one who has to tell everyone.”

It was such a stupid, awful thing to say. He wanted to punch himself the second it left his mouth.

But something flickered in Ayane's eyes—something dark and amused.

For a second, Saburo thought she was going to laugh.

Instead, she stepped down.

Saburo released a breath he didn’t even know he was holding as her feet hit solid concrete again. Ayane leaned back against the metal railing, crossing her arms over her chest.

“You really are pathetic, huh?”

Saburo blinked.

“...What?”

“That's the best excuse you could come up with? You're worried about the attention?”

He opened his mouth—then closed it again, face burning.

“Relax. I wasn't actually gonna jump.”

Liar.

Saburo didn't say it out loud, but the thought echoed in his head.

She had been one second away. He knew that look—he'd seen it in the mirror this morning—and he was unknowingly wearing it right now.

Ayane's eyes flicked over him, like she was sizing him up.

“So why are you up here?”

Saburo held himself back. “No reason.”

Ayane gave him a look of doubt. “You have a girlfriend, don’t you? Having relationship troubles?”

Saburo's heart clenched.

His fingers twitched at the strap of his bag.

“I don't know what you're talking about.”

Ayane's smirk faded.

“Cheating.”

She whispered the word like it tasted bitter on her tongue.

Saburo's stomach twisted.

“...Yeah.”

Ayane let out a low, humorless laugh.

“Guess that makes two of us.”

They didn't talk much after that.

Ayane lit a cigarette but didn't smoke it—just let it burn between her fingers until the wind snuffed it out.

Saburo sat on the opposite side of the rooftop, knees drawn up to his chest. He didn't ask why she was up here. She didn't ask why he was.

It felt weirdly...easy.

Like they'd both found something they weren't supposed to—a secret place where they didn't have to explain themselves.

They stayed there until the bell rang.

Neither of them jumped.

Chapter 2 - Rooftop Therapy

Saburo didn’t tell anyone about the rooftop.

It would've been easy. Just a word to the teachers and they'd lock the door for good. Maybe it was what he should have done.

Instead, he found himself climbing the stairs again the very next day—legs heavy, stomach twisting.

He told himself he just wanted to make sure Ayane wasn’t there.

But when he pushed open the door and saw her leaning against the wall, cigarette dangling between her fingers again, his chest felt... lighter.

Like he'd expected her to be there.

Like he'd wanted her to be there.

“You’re late,” Ayane said without looking at him.

Saburo blinked.

“Late for what?”

Ayane glanced over her shoulder with a lazy smirk.

“Our rooftop therapy session.”

Saburo shifted awkwardly, not sure if she was joking or not.

He walked to the far side of the rooftop—the same spot he'd sat in yesterday—and sank down against the wall.

Neither of them said anything for a long time.

It was strange.

Saburo had never really spoken to Ayane before—not directly. In class, she was always surrounded by girls with perfect hair and perfect makeup, whispering and laughing about things Saburo would never understand.

Boys stared at her like she was some untouchable goddess.

Saburo had stared too, sometimes. He couldn’t help it.

But from his perspective, there was something about her. She looked complex, and oftentimes… lonely.

Even now, leaning against the railing with an unused cigarette burning between her fingers, she looked like someone waiting for something—or maybe for someone—who was never going to come.

"You’re not going to snitch on me, right?" Ayane asked suddenly, breaking the silence.

Saburo blinked.

“What?”

“For coming up here.”

Saburo shifted, picking at the hem of his sleeve.

“I... wasn’t planning on it.”

Ayane’s lips curved—that same teasing little smile she always wore.

“I figured.”

Saburo frowned.

“What’s that supposed to mean?”

Ayane flicked ash onto the concrete.

“You just seem like the kind of guy who wouldn’t say anything. Even if you should.”

There was no bite in her voice. If anything, she sounded... amused.

Saburo's cheeks warmed. He looked down, pretending not to feel the jab.

But he did.

Despite the many times he had heard such things, never had anyone said it so harshly.

As much as he didn’t want to agree… he did.

“...You're right.”

Ayane glanced at him—like maybe she hadn't expected him to agree so easily.

“Huh.”

They fell back into silence.

The wind stirred between them, carrying the smell of cigarette smoke and the distant salt of the sea.

Saburo looked out at the city, over the edge. Just yesterday Ayane had been there, dangerously close.

Even at that moment, a day after the fact, he wondered whether she was actually going to make that choice. Could he have made that choice?  

Finally, Saburo worked up the courage to speak again.

“...Were you really going to do it?”

He didn't have to explain what he meant.

Ayane's fingers twitched around the cigarette.

For a second, he thought she was going to ignore him.

Then she sighed and tilted her head back, eyes half-lidded against the sun.

"I don't know."

It was the most honest answer she could've given.

Saburo didn't press her.

You do know, he thought, but he didn’t dare say it aloud.

“You're not scared of heights?” he asked instead, trying to steer the conversation somewhere less heavy.

Ayane snorted softly.

“I'm scared of everything.”

The words were light, almost playful—but Saburo caught something underneath.

Something bitter.

He glanced at her out of the corner of his eye.

How was she scared of everything when she almost did what he probably never could? It sounded absurd to his ears.

“...You don't seem scared.”

Ayane smirked.

“That's the trick.”

They fell into silence again.

The bell would ring soon.

Saburo should’ve gone back down.

He had no reason to stay.

Except...

“I don’t know who to trust anymore.”

He hadn't meant to say it. The words just spilled out — blunt and heavy, crashing into the open air between them.

Ayane didn't even blink.

“Is this about Emiko?”

Saburo's heart clenched painfully.

“I can’t hide anything from you.”

Ayane flicked her cigarette away, grinding the ember under the toe of her loafer.

“Duh. I'm not blind.”

That should’ve made him angry—how casually she said it, like it was the most obvious thing in the world.

But all Saburo felt was cold.

He hugged his knees tighter to his chest.

“I kept thinking... maybe she was just busy,” he mumbled. “Or stressed about exams... or…”

He trailed off.

Ayane leaned against the railing, arms folded loosely over her chest.

"You wanted to believe her."

Saburo nodded slowly.

Ayane's voice was softer when she spoke again.

“It’s easier, isn't it?”

Saburo swallowed hard.

“Yeah.”

They sat there like that for a long time.

Neither of them said it out loud, but Saburo knew.

She wasn't just talking about him and Emiko.

“You should break up with her.”

The words were blunt — cutting through the silence like a knife.

Saburo flinched.

“I can’t.”

Ayane's eyes flicked toward him, sharp and curious.

“Why not?”

Saburo's mouth opened—then closed again.

He didn't know how to explain it.

How could he tell Ayane that Emiko was the only person who'd ever chosen him? That they'd been together since they were in middle school—before girls like Ayane would even spare him a glance?

How could he explain that holding onto Emiko—even if she was slipping further and further away—was the only thing keeping him from becoming no one?

“...Because I still love her,” he said finally, even though he wasn't sure if it was true.

It probably wasn’t. He couldn’t remember the last time he felt proper love for Emiko.

Ayane's gaze lingered on him for a long moment.

Then she looked away.

“That’s stupid.” Her words were piercing, like she could tell he was forcing himself to lie.

Saburo bristled.

“You wouldn't understand.”

Ayane let out a soft, breathless laugh — the kind that sounded like it hurt more than it healed.

“Yeah.”

Her fingers squeezed together, knuckles white.

“I wouldn't.”

The bell rang below them, muffled and distant.

Neither of them moved.

Saburo glanced sideways at Ayane, watching how the wind tangled in her hair.

Seeing her now, he realized just how fragile she looked. It was like she was one step away from going over the edge.

Perhaps she was already on that path, before Saburo pulled her back. Was it right of him to do so? He couldn’t say.

But regardless of that, something was becoming clear.

All those rumors. All those boys who wanted to date her. All those girls who wanted to be her.

None of them had ever really seen Ayane, had they?

But Saburo saw her now.

And he wondered if maybe she'd been standing on that edge, waiting for someone to notice her all along.

Chapter 3 - Routine

The third day, Ayane wasn’t there.

Saburo sat on the cold concrete alone, knees pulled to his chest. He told himself he didn’t care.

He told himself he wasn’t disappointed.

The fourth day, she was back.

“Morning, watchdog.”

Her voice floated lazily across the rooftop as the door creaked shut behind her. Saburo startled, whipping his head around.

Ayane leaned against the railing, cigarette already between her fingers. She always had one, but Saburo had never actually seen her smoke it.

He wondered if she smoked for real—or if holding the cigarette was just something to do with her hands.

Saburo scowled and looked away, heart settling into something that felt suspiciously like relief.

“I’m not a watchdog.”

Ayane smirked.

“You kind of are, though.”

They fell into the same routine easily, like they'd been meeting here for years instead of days.

Saburo sat by the wall. Ayane leaned on the railing. Neither of them asked why the other kept coming back.

It was strange.

He barely knew her—and she barely knew him—but the silence between them felt... comfortable.

Not like the silence he shared with Emiko these days—just the sight of her texting someone in front of his own eyes made him uneasy.

“Where were you yesterday?”

The words slipped out before he could stop them.

Ayane glanced at him, half-lidded and lazy.

“Miss me?”

Saburo’s ears burned.

Did he miss her? He would be lying if he said no. But they had only been around each other for three days. How could he have formed such an attachment?

Instead, he looked away quickly, muttering, “Just curious.”

Ayane smirked again—but there was something softer underneath it this time.

“Got sick of being around people.”

Saburo blinked.

“Wait... the great Ayane Shirasaki gets sick of people?”

Ayane huffed out a laugh—low and dry.

“People are selfish. They’re always scheming, using others for their own benefit.”

Saburo didn’t respond right away. He glanced at her from the corner of his eye.

“You’re more cynical than I would have thought.”

Ayane frowned, her voice growing cold.

“I don’t remember the last time anyone approached me with genuine intentions. I still have a hard time trusting you.”

A little crack.

A tiny glimpse beneath the perfect, untouchable surface.

Saburo tucked it away carefully, like something fragile he wasn’t supposed to be holding.

“Anyway,” Ayane said, her smirk returning. “What about you, watchdog? How’s life with your cheating girlfriend?”

Saburo flinched.

His chest squeezed painfully—but he wasn’t sure if it was because of the words themselves, or because of how easily Ayane said them.

Like it was already a fact.

Like everyone knew except him.

“...I haven't broken up with her yet.”

Ayane’s smirk faded.

“Seriously? Why the hell not?”

Saburo’s nails dug into the fabric of his slacks.

“It's... complicated.”

Ayane snorted softly, shaking her head.

“Sounds like wimp behavior to me.”

Saburo bristled.

“You don't know anything about it.”

“You're right,” Ayane said, voice light and cutting. “I just know she's been fucking my boyfriend behind your back for months.”

The words hit harder than any slap ever could.

Saburo’s breath caught painfully in his throat.

“You knew.”

“What, that my boyfriend is a cheating ass? Of course I knew.”

She didn’t have to say it like that.

He already knew.

He'd known for weeks now, deep down—even before he'd seen them with his own eyes.

But nobody had ever just said it out loud like that before.

“...Why are you even dating him?”

It came out small and shaky—before Saburo could think better of it.

Who was he to judge her, when he was just as much of a fool himself?

Ayane's fingers twitched around the cigarette.

For a second, she looked like she might actually answer.

Then her smirk snapped back into place—that lazy, detached smile she always wore like a firm piece of armor.

“Why are you still dating her?”

Saburo's mouth opened... then closed again.

He had no argument.

They stared at each other across the rooftop—two stray dogs too scared to chew through their own leashes.

Finally, Ayane looked away.

“...We’re both fools.”

Neither of them said anything after that.

By the time the bell rang, Saburo's chest felt heavier than when he'd come.

But as he walked down the stairs, he realized something strange.

Even though Ayane had torn him open — even though her words still burned like salt in a wound — he felt better.

At least somebody had seen him.

The days started blending together after that.

They never planned to meet.

Saburo would climb the stairs, not sure if she would be there or not.

Some mornings she was. Some mornings she wasn’t.

Some mornings he felt relaxed. Some mornings he didn’t.

Whenever she was there, she always greeted him with the same teasing nickname.

“Morning, watchdog.”

He never smiled. He wouldn’t dare.

But the corners of his mouth twitched sometimes—just a little.

One day, Ayane brought two cans of coffee from the vending machine and wordlessly tossed one to him.

Saburo fumbled to catch it, staring like she'd just handed him a grenade.

“...What's this?”

Ayane shrugged, lighting another unlit cigarette between her fingers.

“You look like shit lately.”

Saburo's throat tightened.

“...You certainly have a way with words.”

Ayane snorted.

“I get that a lot.”

They sat there, drinking in silence.

Neither of them ever mentioned how the coffee cans kept showing up after that.

Little cracks.

Slowly—so slowly he didn't even notice at first—Ayane started talking more.

Not about herself. Not about Naoto.

Never anything too real.

But she made fun of the teachers. Complained about the weather. Grumbled about fake friends who only wanted to hang around her because she knew how to get into clubs.

Saburo just listened.

Some mornings, she'd look at him out of nowhere and ask,

“Do you still love her?”

Saburo would always lie.

Ayane would always smirk like she knew better.

One morning — maybe a week into their strange little routine — Ayane leaned her elbows against the railing and muttered,

“If you break up with Emiko... I'll break up with Naoto.”

Saburo's heart jumped painfully against his ribs.

It was the first time either of them had ever talked about leaving.

He stared at her—half-expecting to see that lazy smirk on her lips again.

But Ayane's face was blank.

Her eyes stayed fixed on the horizon, glassy and far away.

Like she was talking to herself more than to him. Maybe it was always like that and he just never noticed.

“You first,” Saburo said quietly.

Ayane's lips twitched—the ghost of a smile.

“Coward.”

Neither of them ever brought it up again.

The next morning, Ayane wasn't there.

Or the next.

By the fourth day, Saburo's stomach was twisting itself into knots.

He tried not to care.

He told himself it didn't matter.

But when the bell rang for lunch, he found himself climbing the stairs anyway—faster than usual.

The rooftop was empty.

Who was he kidding? Of course it mattered.

Saburo leaned against the wall and waited.

He waited the whole lunch break, staring at the door like some pathetic stray dog waiting for its owner to come home.

The bell rang again.

Still no Ayane.

Saburo walked down the stairs with a knot in his chest so tight it hurt to breathe.

He didn’t know what scared him more—the fact that she was gone.

Or how much he missed her.

Chapter 4 - Missing

Ayane wasn’t there again.

Saburo sat alone on the rooftop for the fifth day in a row, staring at the railing where she always leaned—that same spot where he'd first found her with one foot dangling over the edge.

He hated how empty it looked without her.

He hated how he kept waiting—like some desperate little puppy that didn’t know when to give up.

Who was he to worry about her? She wasn’t his responsibility.

Maybe she finally did it.

The thought slid into his head quietly, uninvited.

Maybe she went back to the edge... and this time, there was no one to stop her.

Wasn’t that what she wanted?

Saburo squeezed his eyes shut.

Don't think like that.

He tried to picture Ayane the way everyone else at school saw her—perfect hair, perfect face, always surrounded by friends. Laughing. Flirting. Untouchable.

It all felt alien now. That wasn’t Ayane.

No, she was simply how only he saw her—imperfect, teasing, distrusting of others. Brash. Fierce. Fragile.

If he told anyone she might be suicidal, they’d probably laugh in his face.

Girls like her don’t kill themselves.

But Saburo had seen what nobody else had.

He'd seen the way her fingers trembled when she thought nobody was looking.

He'd seen the cracks spiraling just beneath that lazy smirk.

He'd seen the hollow, haunted look in her eyes—the one that had mirrored his own.

He couldn't unsee it.

The bell rang.

Saburo stayed sitting for a few seconds longer, staring at the empty railing.

Then he grabbed his bag and headed back inside.

The rest of the day blurred by in a miserable fog.

Saburo couldn't concentrate in class.

His pen scraped over the paper without him noticing a single word.

Every time the classroom door slid open, his head jerked up automatically—searching for long black hair, sharp eyes, that lazy, detached smile.

But she never walked in.

By the time the final bell rang, the knot in his chest was so tight he could barely breathe.

He stuffed his books into his bag and headed straight for the shoe lockers, practically on autopilot—but something stopped him before he could leave.

A group of girls were clustered by the lockers, whispering behind their hands.

Saburo caught one name in the middle of all the gossip.

Ayane Shirasaki.

His heart clenched painfully.

He slowed down without meaning to, pretending to tie his shoe as he listened.

“Did you hear? Ayane hasn’t been to class all week.”

“Yeah, what’s up with that? She’s usually, like... never absent.”

“Maybe she’s sick?”

“Or maybe she finally broke up with Naoto.”

“Ugh, I hope so. That guy’s such a creep.”

“Right? But she’s not dumb—she was probably just using him.”

“Please. Ayane doesn't need to use anyone. She could have any guy she wanted.”

“God, I wish I was her…”

Saburo’s hands curled into fists inside his pockets.

They didn't know a damn thing about her.

They talked about Ayane like she was some kind of goddess—like she was lucky to be who she was.

None of them had ever seen her staring down at the ground from three stories up.

None of them had ever heard the way her voice cracked when she said, “I'm so tired of everything.”

If only they knew.

Saburo forced himself to keep walking before he said something stupid.

He spent the entire train ride home staring at his reflection in the window.

His mind kept circling back to Ayane's last words on the rooftop.

“If you break up with Emiko... I'll break up with Naoto.”

He'd thought she was just messing with him.

But what if she was serious?

What if she was waiting for him to make the first move?

What if she finally got sick of waiting and decided there was nothing left worth sticking around for?

He squeezed his eyes shut and leaned his forehead against the glass, her soft voice echoing in his mind.

“Coward.”

That night, he finally texted Emiko.

It was the first time they'd spoken in days.

Saburo: Can we talk?

It took five minutes for the little typing bubble to appear.

But no response ever came.

The knot in his chest pulled tighter.

He climbed into bed without eating dinner.

He lay awake for hours, staring at the ceiling.

He kept thinking about how small Ayane had looked on the edge of the roof that first day—like a paper doll the wind could carry away at any second.

“I’m so tired of everything.”

What if she really was gone?

What if she was lying cold in some hospital morgue right now—while the whole school gossiped about how perfect she was and Saburo searched for her like some desperate fool?

His stomach twisted violently.

He threw the blankets off and grabbed his phone off the nightstand without thinking.

His fingers hovered over the search bar for a long time before he finally typed,

Ayane Kamizaki... disappearance... news...

Nothing.

He didn't know if that made him feel better or worse.

The next morning, Saburo took the long way to school.

His feet carried him down streets he'd never really paid attention to before—quiet back alleys and narrow roads lined with crumbling old apartment buildings.

He didn't even know what he was looking for until he found it.

On the other side of town, there was a row of concrete docks outlooking the oceanic coast—the kind of place nobody ever went except sailors and old fishermen.

And there she was.

Ayane sat on one of the docks, one foot hanging lazily over the edge.

Her hair was a mess.

Her uniform was rumpled.

She looked like she hadn't slept in days.

Saburo's heart lurched painfully—half relief, half panic.

She was alive.

He almost called out to her—but something made him stop.

For a second, he just stood there at the opposite end of the dock, watching her in silence.

Without all the makeup and the perfect hair, Ayane Kamizaki didn't look like the most popular girl in school anymore.

Unlike her usual school attire, she wore a tank top that exposed her arms. How fragile they looked, under the glowing moonlight.

And she had that same sad expression again.

She looked like any other lonely, miserable kid in this miserable town.

Something that didn’t belong to him.

Chapter 13 - Maelstrom

Chapter 14 - Ambiguous

Ayane’s apartment was cold.

Not in temperature, but in feeling.

Saburo glanced around as he stepped inside.

The space was large, expensive, and pristine, yet it lacked warmth.

No clutter, no mess. No framed photos on the walls, no stray belongings left behind in a rush.

It was the kind of place that looked lived-in only because someone insisted it should be, not because it truly was. It felt more like a display room than a home.

Ayane tossed her bag onto the couch and stretched. “Welcome to my grand palace,” she said dryly, gesturing vaguely around them.

Saburo followed her deeper inside, taking in the sterile neatness. “It’s… big.”

Ayane snorted. “You can just say it’s lifeless. I won’t be offended.”

He hesitated. “You live here alone?”

A small smirk tugged at her lips, but it didn’t reach her eyes. “Most of the time.”

She wandered toward the kitchen, pulling two cans from the fridge before tossing one his way. “Coffee?”

He caught it easily, pressing his fingers into the cold metal. “Thanks.”

Ayane cracked hers open, leaning against the counter as she took a sip. Her gaze lingered on him—sharp, assessing. “You’re awfully quiet.”

Saburo hesitated before speaking. “I just… didn’t expect you to be… rich.”

Her expression didn’t shift, but something in her gaze dimmed, like a light flickering just before it goes out.

“Yeah, well.” She took another sip. “Being rich isn’t as great as it sounds.”

He sat down on the couch, watching as she joined him. They drank in silence for a while, the bitter taste of coffee filling the space between them.

And then, at some point, silence turned into something else.

Something heavier.

Something inevitable.

The coffee was set aside.

It wasn’t Saburo’s first kiss, but it was the first one that made him forget where he was. The first one that felt like falling.

Ayane’s lips were soft, familiar yet unfamiliar, her touch lingering in a way that made his pulse stutter.

It wasn’t desperate. It wasn’t rushed. It just was.

Her hands found their way under his shirt, tracing warmth against his skin. Saburo shuddered. He wasn’t sure when his own hands had started moving, but they were now at her waist, pulling her closer.

All of this was new to him.

He followed Ayane’s lead, but she wasn’t demanding, wasn’t forceful.

She let the moment dictate its own rhythm, and somehow, it was perfect that way.

They weren’t in love—at least, they didn’t seem to be.

But they weren’t careless either.

They moved as though they had already memorized each other’s bodies, as though understanding had been carved into their bones long before this night.

Somewhere between breathless kisses and tangled limbs, Ayane whispered, “Don’t stop.”

So he didn’t.

It was quiet when they finished.

The night had stretched into something slow and weightless, the sounds of the city fading into the distance.

They sat side by side on the bed, barely dressed, sharing their coffees again like nothing had changed—except, of course, everything had.

Their shoulders touched for reasons neither of them tried to name.

For a long time, neither spoke.

Then, Saburo exhaled. “…So.”

Ayane tilted her head slightly, watching him from the corner of her eye.

“What are we?” he asked.

She didn’t answer right away.

“I don’t know,” she finally said. “You tell me.”

Saburo hesitated. “Surely… we’re more than just…” He trailed off, unable to find the right words.

Ayane smirked, her voice laced with something teasing, something unreadable. “Perhaps we’re what some might call ‘fuck-buddies’?”

Saburo flinched at the bluntness. “Ayane…”

She just hummed.

He turned to her fully now, his expression serious. “Do you—”

Before he could finish, she placed a finger against his lips.

“Don’t say it,” she murmured. “I wouldn’t be able to give you an answer.”

Saburo swallowed. “So… that’s it? We’re just… ambiguous?”

Ayane laid her head against his shoulder, exhaling softly. “That’s better than nothing.”

Saburo exhaled through his nose, staring at the ceiling. His body still felt warm where Ayane had touched him, but his mind was tangled, lost in the haze of something neither of them dared to name.

“Better than nothing, huh?” he murmured.

Ayane nodded against his shoulder. “Mm.”

He hesitated before speaking again. “That sounds... lonely.”

Ayane huffed a soft laugh, but it lacked humor. "Maybe. But loneliness is familiar. It’s safe."

Saburo frowned. “Is that all I am to you? Something safe?”

She was quiet for a long moment. Then, in a voice so soft he almost missed it, she admitted, “No.”

Saburo's heart skipped. “Then what am I?”

Ayane didn't move, didn't lift her head. Instead, she reached for his hand, lacing her fingers with his. “You're…” She hesitated, then sighed. “You make things complicated, Saburo.”

“Complicated how?”

“You make me want things I shouldn’t.”

Saburo turned his head slightly, trying to see her face. “Like what?”

Ayane let out a dry chuckle. “Like this." She squeezed his hand lightly, then let go. “Like staying.”

A strange ache settled in his chest. “...You don’t have to leave.”

She finally lifted her head, meeting his gaze with something unreadable. “And then what? We pretend this means nothing?”

“Does it?” Saburo asked, voice steady.

Ayane's lips parted slightly, but no words came.

“Because it doesn't feel like nothing to me,” he admitted.

Her expression faltered, her carefully built walls cracking just a little. “Saburo…”

They fell into silence again, the weight of unspoken things still lingering between them.

Perhaps what they were didn’t need to be said.

Not yet, at least.

Chapter 15 - Unabashed

The rumors weren’t finished with them yet.

In fact, things had become worse seemingly overnight.

By lunchtime the next day, whispers were everywhere.

“Have you seen how Ayane smiles these days?”

“Frankly, it’s a little gross.”

“She’s so two-faced! It probably isn’t even genuine!”

“Yeah, and you know that weird quiet kid, Nakamura? Bet they're screwing.”

“Ugh! He’s practically glued to her!”

The kind of gossip that bounced off Ayane’s armor without leaving a dent.

But Saburo... Saburo felt every word like a stone in his chest.

He sat across from Ayane in the cafeteria, his tray of food untouched. The murmurs pressed in from all directions, suffocating. It was nothing new—he had been invisible for so long, nothing but background noise to his classmates. But now, suddenly, everyone had an opinion about him.

About them.

He glanced up at Ayane. She was eating as if nothing was wrong, movements fluid, expression unreadable. The rumors didn’t touch her.

How can she stay so calm?

“Saburo.”

Her voice snapped him back.

He looked at her. “...Yeah?”

Ayane studied him for a moment, then speared a piece of food with her fork and pointed it at his tray. “Eat.”

He almost wanted to laugh. “Not really hungry.”

She clicked her tongue, setting her fork down. “You're letting them get to you.”

“I'm not,” he lied.

Ayane exhaled, picking up her coffee can and tapping her fingers against the rim. “They're just words.”

“They don't feel like just words.”

A shadow flickered across her face. For a moment, she seemed to want to say something. But before she could, a voice from the next table over cut through the space between them.

“When I look at them, all I see is a master and her dog.”

Snap.

Saburo’s chopsticks broke in half.

The room went dead silent.

Every single person froze—eyes flicking between Saburo and Ayane, waiting to see what would happen. Waiting for the punchline. Waiting to watch him fold.

Saburo didn’t move. He didn’t even breathe.

Ayane stood up so slowly it made his heart stop.

She walked across the room like a lioness stalking through tall grass—one hand tucked in her pocket, coffee can dangling loose from her fingers.

She stopped right in front of the girl who had spoken. Tilted her head just a little.

“What was that?”

The girl swallowed hard—suddenly realizing she might have made a mistake. “N-Nothing…”

Ayane smiled. Sweet. Sharp. “Didn't sound like nothing.”

Her voice was soft—almost playful. But Saburo could feel the crackle in the air—like a wire stretched too tight, ready to snap.

“I was just... telling the truth,” the girl stammered. “We all know you like using people whenever you get the chance!”

A few others murmured in agreement, emboldened.

Ayane let out a breath, almost amused. “So that's the new story, huh? I'm using him now?”

“Everyone knows it.” The girl straightened in her seat, forcing confidence. “You get bored with people, toss them aside. You did it to Naoto, you did it to your old friends. So why wouldn’t you do it to him?”

Saburo clenched his fists under the table.

Ayane let out a hum, tilting her head further. “That’s cute,” she said. “You think you know me?”

The girl scoffed. “Everyone does.”

“Funny.” A beat of silence. “I don’t even know you. But here you are, running your mouth like we’re best friends.”

She leaned in, voice dropping to something softer.

“What was your name again?”

The girl opened her mouth. Nothing came out.

“Thought so.”

Ayane straightened, sipping her coffee. “I could tear you apart right now, you know.”

The girl flinched.

Ayane stepped closer, her smile never wavering. “See, here’s the thing,” she murmured, her voice dipping into something quieter. “You can call me whatever you want. A user. A slut. A monster. If that makes you feel better about yourself, go ahead.”

She tilted her head the other way, her eyes glinting.

“But Saburo?” Her tone sharpened, slicing through the air. “You don’t get to talk about him.”

The girl recoiled like she’d been slapped.

“If you’ve got a problem, then say it to me. But don’t you dare act like he’s nothing.” Ayane’s voice softened again, but there was something in it now—something razor-thin and dangerous.

“Because unlike you, he’s never needed to tear someone else down to feel important.”

The girl’s face burned red. “I—I wasn’t—”

“But you did.”

Ayane cocked her head, her smile stretching just a little wider.

“And you want to know what’s really funny?”

No response.

The girl had nothing left to say.

Ayane’s expression darkened, all traces of amusement gone.

“Saburo is the only one in this entire fucking school who’s unconditionally on my side.”

The air was suffocating.

No one moved.

No one even dared to breathe too loudly.

Saburo gritted his teeth, his grip tightening around the splintered remains of his chopsticks.

She was defending him.

Not ignoring it.

Not brushing it off.

Not laughing it away.

Defending him.

His throat tightened. “Ayane…”

She turned to him.

Then, in front of everyone—without hesitation—she laced her fingers through his.

Saburo’s breath caught.

Her grip was firm. Warm. Unshaken.

She smirked. “You don’t mind being my dog, do you?”

It was teasing. Playful.

But in her eyes, there was something more—something defiant, something unapologetic.

Saburo exhaled through his nose. Slowly, he let his fingers tighten around hers.

“If you’re the one calling me,” he murmured, “then I don’t mind at all.”

Gasps rippled through the cafeteria.

The girl who had spoken shrank in her seat, face burning.

Then, one by one, the murmurs returned—this time, not about Saburo.

Saburo let out a breath he hadn’t realized he was holding. “You didn’t have to do that,” he muttered.

Ayane raised an eyebrow, settling back into her seat. “You wanted me to let them keep talking?”

“No. I just…” He shook his head. “They’re going to keep coming after you.”

Ayane shrugged. “Let them.”

Saburo exhaled through his nose. “You make it sound so easy.”

She set down her fork and leaned forward. “Saburo.”

He met her gaze.

“Do you think I'm using you?”

He blinked. “What? No.”

“Then why does it matter what they say?”

Saburo pressed his lips together, fingers tightening around his tray. “Because it does matter.”

Her expression softened. “To them. Not to me. And not to you.”

He didn’t answer.

Ayane sighed, then smirked. “I’d rather be a villain than a victim.”

Saburo scoffed. “That’s messed up.”

“Is it?” She leaned her chin on her hand. “At least villains get to decide their own stories.”

He shook his head, exasperated. “You really don’t care, huh?”

She twirled a piece of her hair around her finger, smirking. “Oh, I care.”

Saburo stared at her, confused. “...Then why are you smiling?”

Ayane tilted her head, her expression shifting—something rare, something unguarded.

A real smile.

Bright. Unshaken.

Full of complexity.

“Because you’re angry for me.”

Interlude - No More Cowardice

For as long as he lived, Saburo was a coward.

He felt certain ways about certain things, certain people, certain situations. But he had never been brave enough to say any of it out loud. The words always stuck in his throat like a wad of cotton, suffocating him just enough to stay silent.

Saburo didn’t really care one way or another. He figured his cowardice was a part of himself, something he couldn’t change. It was just the way things were. Some people were strong, loud, unafraid to take up space in the world. He wasn’t one of them.

For a while, he believed that.

Even when his only friend, Emiko, cheated on him, he couldn’t do anything. He didn’t scream at her, didn’t demand answers, didn’t let the rage inside him take over. He let it happen. He let himself be walked over. And when she left him behind like an afterthought, he let himself be alone.

That was who he was. Always going to be a coward.

But all of that changed when he met Ayane Shirasaki.

At first, her blunt words upset him.

She had a way of speaking that felt like a scalpel cutting through flesh, exposing the nerves underneath. It was like she could see straight through him, right into the weakness he tried so hard to hide.

But quickly, he realized she wasn’t doing it to hurt him.

She was helping him.

Maybe she was just like him. Perhaps it was easier to point out everyone else’s faults than acknowledge her own.

As Saburo came to know her, he realized Ayane was a deeply complex person. She felt sadness, she felt fear, she felt pain—just like anyone else.

She was not untouchable, not as invincible as she pretended to be.

Beneath the sharp exterior, she was someone who had been hurt too many times, someone who had learned to strike first before the world could wound her again.

And Saburo wanted to protect her.

He wanted to stop others from spouting lies about her.

He wanted to show her ungrateful boyfriend, Naoto, a piece of his mind.

And yet, he couldn’t find the courage to be the one to stand beside her when she needed it most.

Saburo wasn’t stupid.

He knew what she was facing.

The whispers in the hallway, the way people looked at her like she was something to be avoided, something to be feared. He knew she was the subject of cruel gossip, the scapegoat for everyone’s insecurities and petty frustrations.

And she was right—if anyone was going to fight back against them, it had to be her.

But that didn’t make it okay.

Saburo’s stomach twisted with guilt every time he thought about how he had stood silently by, watching her fend for herself. He didn’t want to be a bystander anymore.

Not when it was her.

Still, his fear lingered, crawling into the corners of his mind, telling him that speaking out, even defending Ayane, would be the same as making himself a target. He couldn’t risk it.

So he stayed quiet. He always stayed quiet.

But Ayane wasn’t quiet, was she? She would never sit back and let someone talk behind her back, let someone undermine her, let someone think she was weak.

She didn’t care what they thought of her.

And Saburo?

He was a coward.

A coward who was afraid of what people might say about him if he took a stand. He was scared of being exposed, scared of being seen, and above all, scared of failing her.

But that was the problem—he already was.

He was already failing her by staying silent, by not taking that first step forward.

Saburo clenched his fists, feeling the familiar weight of his cowardice settle like a stone in his chest.

There had been so many moments—so many small chances when Ayane needed someone, and he just stayed there, rooted to the spot, too afraid to move.

But this time… this time, he wouldn’t let it happen again.

He had to change.

He wasn’t sure how, but for the first time in his life, Saburo realized something: cowardice wasn’t a part of him, it was something he had allowed to shape him.

And now, for Ayane—he wasn’t going to let it control him any longer.

“Ayane…” His voice was quiet, hesitant, but she looked at him anyway, her eyes sharp and knowing.

“I want to change.”

Ayane leaned against him, crossing her arms. “Why should you?”

Saburo clenched his fists, thinking back to all of the rumors, the whispers, the cruel nicknames.

“People will never stop talking about us,” he said. “Not unless I—”

“Give me a good reason, Saburo.” Ayane leaned into his face, narrowing her eyes. “Why do you want to change?”

Saburo opened his mouth, then hesitated. Why? Why did he want to be different? Was it just for the rumors? To clear his own name?

No. That wasn’t it.

It was her. It had always been her.

“I want to be able to protect you, Ayane.” The words came out in a rush, his heart pounding. “I want to be strong enough to take care of you for the rest of your life.”

Saburo covered his mouth. He didn’t mean to say all of that. He barely even knew where it had come from.

Ayane stared at him, her eyes wide. Then, slowly, a small smile spread across her lips. A real one.

Her cheeks turned pink, and before he could say anything else, she leaned her head against his shoulder.

“Good answer,” she whispered.

Chapter 16 - One Step Forward

It was late one afternoon when Saburo made his way across the school courtyard, his footsteps echoing in the quiet space.

He approached a particular group of girls, a trio who were known across the school for their love of gossip.

Their entire reputation revolved around the whispers they spread and the rumors they fed.

Anything that could be twisted into something scandalous was a treasure to them, and they never hesitated to pass along the juiciest details.

Saburo had never really talked to them before, never had a reason to. But today was different. Today, he was ready to change things, even if it meant stepping out of his comfort zone.

“Hey, you girls love gossip, don’t you?”

The three girls turned toward him, their eyes lighting up with interest at the sudden attention. Their grins were wide, expectant.

“Yeah, so what?” one of them asked, her voice laced with curiosity.

Saburo smirked, the feeling of power—however small—filling him as he leaned in slightly, lowering his voice just enough to draw them in.

“I’ve got something interesting you might want to check out.” He pulled out his phone and showed them a photo, which captured a certain late-night affair at the subway station.

The girls gasped, their eyes growing wide with excitement as they leaned in closer. One of them stared at him, sizing him up with newfound curiosity.

“What’s your name again?” she asked, still holding her phone out, the photo already saved.

“Saburo,” he replied smoothly. “I’m Ayane’s new boyfriend. And those two in the photo… I think you know who they are.”

A shocked silence fell over them for a moment. Then, just as quickly, their grins widened even more, if that was even possible.

“You’re—?” one of them started, but they didn’t need to finish. Their laughter began to bubble up, eager and hungry for more.

“Tell us everything you know!” they chorused in unison.

Saburo gave a small, satisfied nod, his lips curling into a grin of his own. This was just the beginning.

He had set the stage, and now, the rumors would spread like wildfire.

Later that afternoon, Saburo found himself sitting at a bus stop, his phone in one hand, a can of coffee in the other. The sky above was a muted gray, the sun barely visible behind the clouds, but his mind was far from the weather.

He glanced at his phone screen. 3:00 p.m.

It was a typical after-school hour, the time when the world seemed to pause for a moment before everything picked back up again.

His thumb hovered over the screen as a message from Ayane popped up.

Ayane: How’s it going?

Saburo smiled as his fingers danced across the screen. They had exchanged phone numbers not long ago, a sign of their growing connection.

Saburo: Good. You heard the rumors, right?

A few seconds passed before Ayane’s reply appeared.

Ayane: Yeah. You really created a firestorm.

Saburo chuckled softly, the satisfaction of seeing the consequences of his actions beginning to sink in.

Saburo: Just like we planned.

Ayane: Yep.

Ayane: Good luck. I’m rooting for you.

Saburo nodded to himself, feeling a strange sense of calm mixed with the adrenaline of it all. Before he could respond, he heard footsteps approaching. His eyes flicked up, and his heart skipped when he saw her.

Emiko.

He had known this moment would come. She always took the bus at this time. It was routine. So was the conversation they were about to have, though neither of them knew it yet.

“Saburo…” Emiko’s voice was soft, fragile, her eyes red, as though she had been crying.

“Emiko,” Saburo replied, taking a sip of his coffee, something she had never seen him do before. He stared into the distance for a moment, the silence between them thick with the years of their shared history.

“It’s been a while,” he added, his tone casual, but underneath, there was a coolness that hadn’t been there before.

Emiko reached out, pinching the sleeve of his shirt with a trembling hand. Her face contorted with regret. “I’m sorry,” she whispered, her voice breaking. “I made a mistake—”

Saburo didn’t let her finish. He met her eyes, and for the first time, he let the anger he'd been holding in flood his voice.

“Which one? Kissing Naoto when you thought I wasn’t watching? Or going to bed with Naoto behind my back?”

Emiko flinched, her face pale as she recoiled slightly from his words.

This was a side to Saburo she had never seen before. He wasn’t the quiet, shy boy she once knew.

His words were sharp, cutting through the layers of what had been left unsaid for so long.

And he didn’t flinch when talking about such vulgar things anymore.

“I…” Emiko’s voice faltered as she trembled. “I wanted to talk with you. I truly did. But… I got scared.”

Saburo frowned, his eyes narrowing. “Even when I texted you and you never responded once?”

Emiko hesitated, guilt flooding her face. “Y-yes…”

He looked away, letting the silence hang in the air. His heart ached for her, but there was nothing left to say. He couldn’t fix this.

“Do you love him?” he asked, his voice steady, though the question weighed heavily on him.

Emiko nodded hesitantly, her eyes avoiding his. “…I do.”

Saburo’s lips tightened, and for a moment, he was at a loss for words.

He wanted to say so many things, warn her about Naoto, but something held him back. Perhaps it was better if she discovered it herself.

That would be his final revenge—letting her walk into the same trap that had ensnared him.

Instead, he changed the subject, letting the silence settle between them.

“I suppose the whole school is talking about you now?”

Emiko frowned, her gaze lowering to the ground. “Everyone’s calling me a cheater…” Her voice quivered as her body shook with each word. “And I can’t deny it because it’s true.”

Saburo smiled, though it wasn’t a smile of satisfaction. It was sad, almost bittersweet. He couldn’t bring himself to enjoy her misery, but there was a small part of him that couldn’t help but feel vindicated.

“Now you know what it feels like to be betrayed by someone you trusted,” he whispered, his voice low.

Emiko’s head snapped up, her eyes wide with disbelief. “W-what?”

Saburo’s voice dropped, cold and final.

“Consider the rumors a parting gift from me,” he said. “I’m breaking up with you, Emiko.”

The words hung in the air, and for a moment, it felt like time itself had frozen. Emiko stood there, her body stiff and unmoving. Her eyes were wide, unable to process what he had just said.

She slowly looked down, her face pale, as if searching for the right words, but they never came.

“Saburo,” she muttered finally, her voice barely a whisper. “You always were a good friend. I never minded being around you. That’s why I thought I could be happy if we were together. But…”

Her words choked off as tears began to fall, silently tracking down her cheeks. "All I did was hurt you. I never thought about what you wanted, and… I ended up hurting myself too."

She looked up at him, her gaze meeting his with an unexpected calmness. Through her tears, she smiled faintly. “I’m so sorry, Saburo. I can see now… I was never the one for you.”

Saburo’s heart twisted, and he brushed a strand of her hair from her face, his smile sad and resigned. He still felt bad for her, but this was what he wanted.

Revenge, in some small way, was satisfying. But it was still bitter.

Later that day, Saburo entered the rooftop, his footsteps light as he approached Ayane. She was sitting there, watching the sunset, a can of coffee in her hand.

She turned toward him, tilting her head as her eyes met his.

“So?” she asked, her voice teasing but expectant.

Saburo smiled, though the expression didn’t quite reach his eyes.

“I broke up with her,” he said, his voice a mix of relief and resignation.

Ayane’s eyes widened. “You actually did it.”

Saburo raised an eyebrow, feigning offense. “You doubted me?”

Ayane chuckled, shaking her head as she pulled him into a hug. “A little,” she admitted. “I guess I can’t call you a coward anymore.”

“That’s a relief,” Saburo muttered, his own voice soft with a quiet satisfaction.

But Ayane pulled back slightly, a thoughtful expression crossing her face. “Don’t get too relaxed,” she teased, her voice playful. “I’m not going to start being all cutesy now that you’ve changed yourself.”

Saburo smirked. “What about after you break up with Naoto?”

Everything in the air froze. Ayane’s smile disappeared, her posture tensing. She stepped back, turning her back to him, her shoulders stiff.

“…Ayane?” Saburo called out, his voice filled with concern. “Did I say something wrong?”

Ayane didn’t answer immediately. When she did, her voice was quieter, more guarded.

“Breaking up with Naoto is not as easy as it sounds.”

Saburo studied her carefully, noticing the tension in her shoulders as she turned her back to him, her fingers curling into fists at her sides.

He took a step closer, reaching out slightly, but he hesitated before touching her. Instead, he spoke softly, trying to offer reassurance without pushing too hard.

“I didn't mean to make it sound so simple,” he said. “But you're not the kind of person who would stay in a relationship that hurts you, are you?”

Ayane let out a long, almost frustrated sigh. She didn't turn to face him, but Saburo could hear the vulnerability in her voice when she finally spoke.

“You don't get it, Saburo,” she murmured, her words heavy with a weariness that cut through the air. “It's not just about being hurt. It's about... everything—the lies, the betrayal, the expectations. Naoto... he's not just some guy. He's the one everyone expects me to be with. The one everyone thinks I should be with.”

Saburo frowned, his brow furrowing in confusion. He stepped closer, his voice firm yet gentle as he spoke.

“But it’s not about what everyone expects. It’s about what you want. Don’t you want to be free from all that pressure?”

Ayane didn’t respond immediately.

The silence between them stretched on, and Saburo could feel the weight of her struggle, even without her words.

She was caught in a web of expectations—something he couldn't entirely comprehend, but he understood enough to know it was holding her back.

Finally, Ayane turned to face him. Her eyes were filled with frustration, but also a deep sadness.

“Of course I want to be free,” she admitted, her voice barely above a whisper.

“But what if breaking free means I lose everything? What if everything I’ve known, everyone I’ve known, turns their backs on me? I’m not sure I’m ready for that kind of change. Not yet.”

Saburo didn’t know what to say.

He could sense the depth of Ayane’s conflict, the fear that came with the idea of cutting ties with Naoto, even though he wasn’t the right person for her.

This wasn’t about standing up to something that was clearly wrong—this was about confronting an overwhelming emotional burden that was rooted in years of expectations and entanglements.

Nothing he thought of saying seemed right. She was faced with an incredibly difficult decision, one he couldn’t easily help with.

It wasn’t something he could solve with a simple answer. He couldn’t just tell her to walk away and expect it to be easy. It was too complicated, too layered with emotion and history.

And yet, he didn’t want her to feel like she had to carry this alone.

“…you promised me,” Saburo said quietly, breaking the silence. He extended his hand toward her, his voice soft but steady. “You said that if I broke up with Emiko... then you would break up with Naoto.”

Ayane’s response was slow, almost reluctant, as if the words were hard to come by. After a long pause, she sighed deeply, her shoulders slumping as she turned her gaze downward. “I say some crazy things, don’t I?”

Saburo smiled, his expression warm despite the seriousness of the moment. “Yes. But I like hearing you talk.”

Ayane frowned, but there was no malice in the gesture. She approached him and, without a word, rested her head on his shoulder.

The simple gesture was a quiet confession, an admission of the vulnerability she had been hiding. “I’ll break up with him,” she whispered, her voice barely audible. “One day.”

Saburo smiled, his heart swelling with a quiet sense of hope. He wrapped his arm around her gently, his voice filled with quiet determination.

“I can wait,” he said softly, knowing that no matter what happened next, he would be there for her, every step of the way.

Chapter 17 - Rainfall

The next day, the school yet again buzzed with whispers of Saburo and Ayane.

The rumors had already begun to spiral like wildfire, and the school’s social atmosphere had shifted in ways Saburo hadn’t fully anticipated.
